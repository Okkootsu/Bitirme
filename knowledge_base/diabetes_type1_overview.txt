Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus - Comprehensive Overview
Sources: American Diabetes Association (ADA) Standards of Care 2024, WHO, JDRF, Endocrine Society

DEFINITION AND PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

Type 1 diabetes mellitus (T1DM) is a chronic autoimmune disease characterized by the destruction of insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreatic islets of Langerhans. This destruction is mediated by autoreactive T lymphocytes and leads to absolute insulin deficiency. Without exogenous insulin replacement, T1DM is fatal.

The autoimmune process involves:
- CD4+ and CD8+ T-cell mediated destruction of beta cells
- Presence of autoantibodies: GAD65 (glutamic acid decarboxylase), IA-2 (insulinoma-associated antigen-2), ZnT8 (zinc transporter 8), and insulin autoantibodies (IAA)
- Genetic susceptibility linked to HLA-DR3, HLA-DR4, and HLA-DQ genotypes
- Environmental triggers suspected: viral infections (Coxsackie B, enteroviruses), early cow's milk exposure, vitamin D deficiency

The preclinical phase can last months to years. Clinical symptoms appear when approximately 80-90% of beta cells are destroyed.

EPIDEMIOLOGY

- Accounts for 5-10% of all diabetes cases worldwide
- Peak incidence in children aged 10-14 years, but can occur at any age
- Incidence is increasing globally by approximately 3-4% per year
- Highest incidence rates in Finland (>60 per 100,000/year) and Sardinia
- In Turkey, the incidence of T1DM in children (0-14 years) is approximately 10-12 per 100,000/year (Turkish Society of Endocrinology and Metabolism data)

CLASSIFICATION

- Type 1A (Immune-mediated): Most common form, positive autoantibodies
- Type 1B (Idiopathic): No evidence of autoimmunity, more common in African and Asian populations
- Latent Autoimmune Diabetes in Adults (LADA): Slowly progressive autoimmune diabetes in adults, often initially misdiagnosed as Type 2

CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic symptoms (often acute onset):
- Polyuria (excessive urination)
- Polydipsia (excessive thirst)
- Polyphagia (excessive hunger) with weight loss
- Fatigue and weakness
- Blurred vision

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) at presentation:
- Occurs in 20-40% of children at diagnosis
- Symptoms: nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, Kussmaul breathing (deep rapid breathing), fruity breath odor, altered consciousness
- DKA criteria: blood glucose >250 mg/dL, arterial pH <7.3, serum bicarbonate <18 mEq/L, ketonemia/ketonuria
- Medical emergency requiring immediate treatment with IV fluids and insulin

DIAGNOSIS

Diagnostic criteria (same as Type 2, but context differs):
- Fasting Plasma Glucose ≥126 mg/dL (7.0 mmol/L)
- 2-hour OGTT ≥200 mg/dL (11.1 mmol/L)
- HbA1c ≥6.5%
- Random glucose ≥200 mg/dL with classic symptoms

Distinguishing from Type 2:
- Autoantibody testing (GAD65, IA-2, ZnT8, IAA)
- C-peptide levels (low or undetectable in T1DM)
- Age of onset (typically younger, but not exclusively)
- Absence of obesity-related features (though obesity can coexist)

TREATMENT

Insulin Therapy (required for survival):
- Basal-bolus regimen: Long-acting insulin (glargine, detemir, degludec) + rapid-acting insulin (lispro, aspart, glulisine) before meals
- Insulin pump therapy (CSII - Continuous Subcutaneous Insulin Infusion): Delivers rapid-acting insulin continuously with boluses for meals
- Hybrid closed-loop systems ("artificial pancreas"): Automated insulin delivery based on CGM readings

Blood Glucose Monitoring:
- Self-monitoring of blood glucose (SMBG): 4-10 times daily
- Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM): Real-time glucose readings every 1-5 minutes
- Key metrics: Time in Range (TIR 70-180 mg/dL, target >70%), Time Below Range (<70 mg/dL, target <4%)

Glycemic Targets:
- HbA1c <7.0% for most adults (ADA recommendation)
- HbA1c <7.5% for children and adolescents
- Individualized targets based on hypoglycemia risk, life expectancy, comorbidities

COMPLICATIONS SPECIFIC TO T1DM

- Hypoglycemia: Most common acute complication of insulin therapy
- Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA): Can occur with illness, insulin omission, or pump failure
- Higher risk of celiac disease (6-8% prevalence vs 1% general population)
- Higher risk of autoimmune thyroid disease (15-30%)
- Increased cardiovascular risk (2-4x compared to general population)

EMERGING THERAPIES

- Teplizumab (FDA-approved 2022): Anti-CD3 monoclonal antibody that can delay onset of clinical T1DM by approximately 2 years in high-risk individuals
- Islet cell transplantation: Can restore insulin production but requires immunosuppression
- Stem cell-derived beta cells: Under clinical investigation
- Immunotherapy combinations: Targeting multiple immune pathways to preserve beta cell function
