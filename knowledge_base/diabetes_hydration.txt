Hydration and Diabetes
Source: European Journal of Clinical Nutrition; American Diabetes Association; Kidney International Reports

WHY HYDRATION MATTERS IN DIABETES

Proper hydration is critical for people with diabetes. When blood sugar is high, the kidneys work overtime to filter and absorb excess glucose. When they cannot keep up, the excess sugar is excreted into urine, pulling fluids from tissues. This causes increased urination (polyuria) and thirst (polydipsia), two hallmark symptoms of diabetes. Dehydration can further concentrate blood sugar, creating a dangerous cycle.

Key facts:
- High blood sugar (above 180 mg/dL) triggers glucose excretion in urine
- Each gram of excreted glucose pulls approximately 15 mL of water with it
- Dehydration can raise blood sugar by 50-100 mg/dL
- Chronic mild dehydration is associated with higher HbA1c levels
- Adequate hydration improves insulin sensitivity by 5-10%
- Dehydration is a trigger for diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) and hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (HHS)

DAILY WATER REQUIREMENTS

General recommendation: 30-35 mL per kg of body weight per day.
- 70 kg person: approximately 2.1-2.5 liters (8-10 glasses) daily
- Increase by 500 mL for each hour of moderate exercise
- Increase in hot weather, at high altitude, or during illness
- Increase if blood sugar is running high (helps kidneys flush excess glucose)

Signs of dehydration:
- Dark yellow urine (aim for pale yellow)
- Dry mouth and lips
- Headache and fatigue
- Dizziness when standing
- Decreased urine output
- Elevated blood sugar without other explanation
- In severe cases: confusion, rapid heartbeat, sunken eyes

BEST BEVERAGES FOR DIABETIC PATIENTS

Water: The best choice. No calories, no carbs, no effect on blood sugar. Add lemon, cucumber, or mint for flavor without sugar.

Unsweetened tea: Green tea may improve insulin sensitivity. Black tea contains antioxidants. Herbal teas (chamomile, peppermint) are calorie-free. Avoid adding sugar or honey.

Coffee: Black coffee has minimal effect on blood sugar. 3-4 cups daily may reduce type 2 diabetes risk by 25%. Avoid sugary coffee drinks (lattes, frappuccinos can contain 50-70g sugar). Caffeine can temporarily raise blood sugar in some individuals.

BEVERAGES TO AVOID OR LIMIT

Fruit juice: Even 100% fruit juice spikes blood sugar rapidly. 250 mL orange juice contains approximately 26g sugar. Whole fruit is always better (fiber slows absorption).

Regular soda: A single 330 mL can contains 35-40g sugar. Associated with increased diabetes risk and worsened control. One of the worst choices for blood sugar management.

Diet soda: Zero calories but controversial. Some studies suggest artificial sweeteners may affect gut microbiome. May increase cravings for sweet foods. Acceptable in moderation but water is preferred.

Energy drinks: High caffeine plus sugar or artificial sweeteners. Can cause rapid blood sugar spikes and crashes. Associated with cardiovascular risks, especially in diabetes.

HYDRATION DURING EXERCISE

- Drink 250-500 mL water 2 hours before exercise
- Sip 150-250 mL every 15-20 minutes during exercise
- After exercise: replace fluids based on weight loss (1 kg lost equals 1 liter to replace)
- For exercise over 60 minutes: consider electrolyte drinks (sugar-free versions)
- Check blood sugar before, during, and after exercise

KIDNEY PROTECTION THROUGH HYDRATION

Adequate hydration protects kidney function in diabetes:
- Helps kidneys filter waste products efficiently
- Reduces risk of urinary tract infections (more common in diabetes)
- May slow progression of diabetic kidney disease
- Prevents kidney stone formation
- Patients with advanced kidney disease may need to restrict fluids (follow nephrologist guidance)
