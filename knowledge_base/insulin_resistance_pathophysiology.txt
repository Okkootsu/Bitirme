Insulin Resistance and Type 2 Diabetes Pathophysiology
Sources: DeFronzo RA "From the Triumvirate to the Ominous Octet" (Diabetes 2009), ADA Position Statement, Endocrine Reviews

INSULIN RESISTANCE - DEFINITION

Insulin resistance (IR) is a pathological condition in which cells fail to respond normally to the hormone insulin. Higher-than-normal levels of insulin are needed to maintain normal blood glucose levels. Insulin resistance is the hallmark feature of Type 2 diabetes and precedes the development of overt hyperglycemia by years to decades.

THE OMINOUS OCTET (DeFronzo Model)

Professor Ralph DeFronzo described eight pathophysiological defects contributing to hyperglycemia in T2DM:

1. Decreased insulin secretion (Beta cell dysfunction):
- Progressive loss of beta cell function is the primary determinant of disease progression
- At diagnosis, approximately 50% of beta cell function is already lost
- Mechanisms: glucotoxicity, lipotoxicity, amyloid deposition (islet amyloid polypeptide/IAPP), oxidative stress, ER stress, inflammatory cytokines
- Beta cell mass decreases by approximately 1-2% per year

2. Increased hepatic glucose production (Liver):
- The liver produces excessive glucose via gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis
- Insulin normally suppresses hepatic glucose output; in IR, this suppression is impaired
- Contributes significantly to fasting hyperglycemia
- Metformin primarily targets this defect

3. Decreased peripheral glucose uptake (Muscle):
- Skeletal muscle is responsible for ~75-80% of insulin-stimulated glucose disposal
- GLUT4 transporter translocation to cell surface is impaired
- Reduced glycogen synthesis
- Major contributor to postprandial hyperglycemia
- Exercise and TZDs improve muscle glucose uptake

4. Increased lipolysis (Adipose tissue):
- Insulin normally suppresses lipolysis; in IR, free fatty acid (FFA) release is increased
- Elevated FFAs worsen insulin resistance in muscle and liver (Randle cycle/lipotoxicity)
- Ectopic fat deposition (liver, muscle, pancreas) further impairs insulin signaling
- Visceral adiposity is more metabolically harmful than subcutaneous fat

5. Decreased incretin effect (GI tract):
- Incretin hormones (GLP-1, GIP) are responsible for 50-70% of postprandial insulin secretion
- In T2DM, GLP-1 secretion may be reduced, and the insulinotropic effect of GIP is diminished
- This "incretin defect" reduces meal-stimulated insulin response
- GLP-1 receptor agonists and DPP-4 inhibitors target this pathway

6. Increased glucagon secretion (Alpha cells):
- Paradoxical hyperglucagonemia in T2DM (glucagon is not appropriately suppressed after meals)
- Stimulates hepatic glucose production
- GLP-1 suppresses glucagon secretion

7. Increased glucose reabsorption (Kidney):
- SGLT2 transporters in proximal tubule reabsorb ~90% of filtered glucose
- In diabetes, SGLT2 expression is upregulated, increasing the renal threshold for glycosuria
- The kidney effectively "returns" more glucose to the bloodstream
- SGLT2 inhibitors block this pathway, causing therapeutic glycosuria

8. Neurotransmitter dysfunction (Brain):
- Central nervous system resistance to insulin and leptin
- Dysregulated appetite and energy homeostasis
- Impaired hypothalamic glucose sensing
- GLP-1 RAs have central appetite-suppressing effects

MOLECULAR MECHANISMS OF INSULIN RESISTANCE

Normal Insulin Signaling:
Insulin → Insulin Receptor (tyrosine kinase) → IRS-1/IRS-2 phosphorylation → PI3K activation → Akt/PKB activation → GLUT4 translocation → Glucose uptake

Key disruptions:
- Serine phosphorylation of IRS-1 (instead of tyrosine) → blocks signal
- Inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6) activate JNK and IKKβ pathways → serine phosphorylation
- Diacylglycerol (DAG) accumulation → activates PKC → serine phosphorylation
- Ceramide accumulation → inhibits Akt
- ER stress → activates unfolded protein response (UPR) → insulin resistance
- Mitochondrial dysfunction → increased reactive oxygen species (ROS)

METABOLIC SYNDROME

Insulin resistance is the central feature of metabolic syndrome, defined by (ATP III criteria, 3 of 5):
- Waist circumference: >102 cm (men), >88 cm (women)
- Triglycerides: ≥150 mg/dL
- HDL cholesterol: <40 mg/dL (men), <50 mg/dL (women)
- Blood pressure: ≥130/85 mmHg
- Fasting glucose: ≥100 mg/dL

Metabolic syndrome affects approximately 20-25% of the global adult population and increases the risk of T2DM by 5-fold and cardiovascular disease by 2-fold.

MEASURING INSULIN RESISTANCE

Research methods:
- Hyperinsulinemic-euglycemic clamp: Gold standard, measures glucose disposal rate under controlled insulin infusion
- Frequently Sampled IV Glucose Tolerance Test (FSIVGTT): Minimal model analysis

Clinical proxies:
- HOMA-IR (Homeostatic Model Assessment): Fasting insulin (μU/mL) × Fasting glucose (mg/dL) / 405
  * Normal: <1.0, IR likely: >2.0, Significant IR: >2.5
- Triglyceride/HDL ratio: >3.0 suggests insulin resistance
- Fasting insulin levels: >25 μU/mL suggests significant IR

PROGRESSION FROM INSULIN RESISTANCE TO DIABETES

Stage 1 - Normal glucose tolerance with compensatory hyperinsulinemia:
- IR is present but beta cells compensate by increasing insulin production
- Fasting and postprandial glucose remain normal
- Elevated fasting insulin levels

Stage 2 - Impaired glucose tolerance (Prediabetes):
- Beta cell compensation begins to fail
- Fasting glucose: 100-125 mg/dL (Impaired Fasting Glucose)
- 2-hour OGTT glucose: 140-199 mg/dL (Impaired Glucose Tolerance)
- HbA1c: 5.7-6.4%
- Annual conversion rate to diabetes: 5-10%

Stage 3 - Overt Type 2 diabetes:
- Significant beta cell failure
- Fasting glucose ≥126 mg/dL and/or HbA1c ≥6.5%
- Progressive decline in beta cell function if untreated

GENETIC AND ENVIRONMENTAL FACTORS

Genetic susceptibility:
- Over 400 genetic loci associated with T2DM risk
- Key genes: TCF7L2 (strongest association), PPARG, KCNJ11, ABCC8, FTO (obesity)
- Concordance rate in monozygotic twins: 70-90% (much higher than T1DM at 30-50%)
- Family history: 40% risk if one parent has T2DM, 70% if both parents

Environmental factors:
- Obesity (especially visceral): Primary modifiable risk factor
- Physical inactivity: Sedentary lifestyle reduces insulin sensitivity
- Diet: High-calorie, high-fat, high-sugar diets promote IR
- Sleep deprivation: <6 hours/night increases IR and diabetes risk
- Chronic stress: Cortisol elevation promotes IR
- Gut microbiome: Dysbiosis linked to systemic inflammation and IR
- Endocrine disruptors: BPA, phthalates may contribute to IR
