Diabetes and Dental Health: Oral Complications and Care
Source: American Dental Association; International Diabetes Federation; Journal of Periodontology

THE DIABETES-ORAL HEALTH CONNECTION

Diabetes and oral health have a bidirectional relationship. High blood sugar creates an environment that promotes bacterial growth in the mouth, while severe gum disease can make blood sugar harder to control. People with diabetes are 2-3 times more likely to develop periodontal (gum) disease. Periodontal disease is now considered the "sixth complication" of diabetes.

Key facts:
- 22% of diabetic patients have periodontal disease
- Severe gum disease can increase HbA1c by 0.4%
- Treatment of periodontal disease can reduce HbA1c by 0.4%
- Diabetic patients lose more teeth than non-diabetic individuals
- Dry mouth (xerostomia) affects 40-80% of diabetic patients

COMMON ORAL COMPLICATIONS

Periodontal disease: The most serious oral complication. Begins as gingivitis (red, swollen, bleeding gums) and can progress to periodontitis (bone loss, tooth loosening). High glucose in saliva feeds bacteria that form plaque. Impaired immune response in diabetes slows healing.

Dry mouth (xerostomia): Reduced saliva production due to high blood sugar and certain medications (metformin, antihypertensives). Saliva protects against bacteria, so dry mouth increases cavity risk. Can cause difficulty swallowing, altered taste, and mouth sores.

Oral thrush (candidiasis): Fungal infection appearing as white patches on tongue and inner cheeks. High glucose in saliva promotes Candida growth. More common with dentures, smoking, and antibiotic use.

Burning mouth syndrome: Tingling or burning sensation in the mouth, often related to diabetic neuropathy. May affect taste perception and food enjoyment.

Delayed wound healing: Tooth extractions and oral surgeries heal slower in diabetic patients. Infection risk is higher. Blood sugar should be well-controlled before elective dental procedures.

DAILY ORAL CARE ROUTINE

Essential practices for diabetic patients:
- Brush teeth at least twice daily with fluoride toothpaste
- Use a soft-bristled toothbrush, replace every 3 months
- Floss daily to remove plaque between teeth
- Use antimicrobial mouthwash (chlorhexidine if prescribed)
- Stay hydrated to combat dry mouth
- Chew sugar-free gum to stimulate saliva production
- Avoid smoking: multiplies periodontal disease risk by 5x in diabetics
- Check mouth daily for sores, white patches, or bleeding gums

DENTAL VISIT RECOMMENDATIONS

- Visit dentist every 3-6 months (more frequently than general population)
- Inform dentist about diabetes status, medications, and latest HbA1c
- Schedule appointments in the morning when blood sugar is most stable
- Eat normally and take medications before appointments
- Bring glucose tablets to appointments in case of hypoglycemia
- For surgical procedures: optimize blood sugar (fasting glucose below 180 mg/dL)
- Antibiotic prophylaxis may be needed for invasive procedures if blood sugar is poorly controlled
