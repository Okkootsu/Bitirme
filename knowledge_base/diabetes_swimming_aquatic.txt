Swimming and Aquatic Exercise for Diabetes Management
Source: ADA Exercise Guidelines; Aquatic Exercise Association Standards; Journal of Physical Activity and Health 2022

BENEFITS OF SWIMMING FOR PEOPLE WITH DIABETES

Swimming and aquatic exercise offer unique advantages for individuals with diabetes, particularly those who face barriers to land-based exercise due to obesity, arthritis, peripheral neuropathy, or foot ulcers. Water buoyancy reduces body weight bearing by 50 to 90 percent depending on immersion depth, dramatically lowering joint stress while providing 12 to 14 times greater resistance than air for muscle conditioning. A 2022 meta-analysis of 14 studies found that regular swimming (3 times per week for 12 or more weeks) reduces HbA1c by 0.31 percentage points, fasting glucose by 16 mg/dL, and systolic blood pressure by 6 mmHg. Swimming engages all major muscle groups simultaneously, resulting in caloric expenditure of 400 to 700 kcal per hour depending on intensity and stroke type.

TYPES OF AQUATIC EXERCISE PROGRAMS

Lap swimming at moderate intensity (60 to 70 percent of maximum heart rate) for 30 to 60 minutes is the most studied aquatic exercise for diabetes. Water aerobics classes, typically conducted in chest-deep water, combine cardiovascular training with resistance movements and are particularly suitable for older adults and those new to exercise. Aqua jogging using a flotation belt allows running mechanics without ground impact, achieving comparable heart rate and oxygen consumption to land-based jogging at approximately 75 percent of the metabolic cost. Water-based resistance training using foam dumbbells, resistance paddles, or water currents effectively builds muscle strength while accommodating limited mobility.

GLYCEMIC RESPONSE TO AQUATIC EXERCISE

Water temperature significantly influences the glycemic response to swimming. Cool water (below 27 degrees Celsius) stimulates catecholamine release and may initially raise blood glucose, while warm water (30 to 34 degrees Celsius) promotes vasodilation and can accelerate insulin absorption from subcutaneous injection sites, potentially causing hypoglycemia. Thermoneutral water temperature for exercise is 28 to 30 degrees Celsius. Blood glucose should be measured before entering the pool, and sessions should be avoided if glucose is below 100 mg/dL without carbohydrate intake or above 300 mg/dL. Fast-acting carbohydrates must be kept poolside.

INSULIN PUMPS AND WATERPROOF CONSIDERATIONS

Modern insulin pumps vary in their waterproof ratings. Some pumps like the Omnipod are waterproof to IPX8 standards (submersion up to 7.6 meters for 60 minutes), while most tubed pumps should be disconnected before swimming. When disconnecting a pump for swimming, the session should not exceed 1 to 2 hours to prevent excessive hyperglycemia from interrupted basal insulin delivery. A small correction bolus may be needed upon reconnection. Continuous glucose monitors (CGMs) also vary in water resistance; the Dexcom G7 and Freestyle Libre 3 are rated for submersion up to 1 meter for 30 minutes. Adhesive patches or waterproof overlays can help secure sensors during swimming.

POOL SAFETY AND HYGIENE

Individuals with diabetes should never swim alone due to the risk of hypoglycemia-induced disorientation or loss of consciousness. A swimming buddy or lifeguard who is aware of the swimmer's diabetes status and trained to recognize hypoglycemia symptoms is essential. Open wounds, including active foot ulcers or recent injection sites that are irritated, increase infection risk in pool water and should be covered with waterproof dressings. Chlorinated pool water can dry the skin, so thorough rinsing and moisturizing after swimming is important, particularly for the feet. Proper footwear should be worn on the pool deck to prevent foot injuries.

STRUCTURED AQUATIC PROGRAMS

A beginner aquatic program should start with 15 to 20 minutes of water walking or gentle swimming, progressing to 30 to 45 minutes over 4 to 6 weeks. A sample session includes a 5-minute warm-up of gentle water walking, 20 to 30 minutes of lap swimming or structured aquatic exercises, and a 5-minute cool-down. In Turkey, many belediye (municipal) swimming pools and rehabilitation centers offer programs suitable for individuals with chronic conditions. The Turkish Diabetes Foundation (Turkiye Diyabet Vakfi) has partnered with several aquatic centers to provide supervised exercise programs for diabetes patients.

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS

Individuals with autonomic neuropathy may have impaired thermoregulation and should avoid extremes of water temperature. Those with cardiovascular disease should have medical clearance and cardiac stress testing before beginning a swimming program. Peripheral neuropathy patients must carefully dry and inspect feet after every swim session. Contact lens wearers should use goggles to prevent eye infections. Diabetic gastroparesis may cause nausea during prone swimming positions, and adjusting meal timing to at least 2 hours before swimming is recommended. Dehydration can occur even in water, so adequate fluid intake before, during breaks, and after swimming is necessary.
