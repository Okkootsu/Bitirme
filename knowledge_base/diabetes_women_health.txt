Diabetes and Women's Health: PCOS, Menopause, HRT, Cancer Screening, Bone Health
Source: ADA Standards of Care 2024; Endocrine Society Women and Diabetes Guidelines; WHO Women's Health Reports

OVERVIEW

Diabetes affects women differently than men due to hormonal fluctuations, reproductive
health considerations, and gender-specific complications. Women with diabetes face unique
challenges across their lifespan from polycystic ovary syndrome in reproductive years to
menopause-related metabolic changes. Globally, approximately 204 million women live with
diabetes, projected to rise to 308 million by 2045. Women with diabetes have a 40% greater
risk of coronary heart disease compared to diabetic men, and diabetes erases much of the
cardiovascular protection typically afforded to premenopausal women.

POLYCYSTIC OVARY SYNDROME AND DIABETES

PCOS affects 6-12% of women of reproductive age and is strongly linked to insulin resistance
and Type 2 diabetes. Women with PCOS have a 3-5 times higher risk of developing Type 2
diabetes. Common features include irregular menstrual cycles, hyperandrogenism (acne,
hirsutism, hair loss), obesity, and infertility. Metformin is commonly used to address insulin
resistance in PCOS, improving menstrual regularity and ovulation. Lifestyle modifications
including 5-10% weight loss can significantly improve metabolic and reproductive outcomes.
Annual glucose screening with OGTT is recommended for all women with PCOS.

MENSTRUAL CYCLE AND BLOOD GLUCOSE

Hormonal fluctuations throughout the menstrual cycle affect blood glucose levels. Estrogen
generally improves insulin sensitivity while progesterone promotes insulin resistance. Many
women experience higher blood glucose in the luteal phase (1-2 weeks before menstruation).
Tracking blood glucose alongside menstrual cycles helps identify patterns. Insulin doses
may need adjustment during different cycle phases. Premenstrual syndrome symptoms can
be confused with hypoglycemia symptoms.

MENOPAUSE AND DIABETES

Menopause brings significant metabolic changes that complicate diabetes management.
Declining estrogen leads to increased insulin resistance, central fat redistribution, higher
cardiovascular risk, and bone density loss. Hot flashes and night sweats can be confused
with hypoglycemia episodes. Sleep disturbances worsen glycemic control. Hormone
replacement therapy (HRT) may improve insulin sensitivity and glycemic control in some
women, but risks and benefits must be individualized. HRT decisions should consider
cardiovascular risk, breast cancer history, and thromboembolism risk.

BREAST CANCER SCREENING

Women with Type 2 diabetes have a 20-27% increased risk of breast cancer. Proposed
mechanisms include hyperinsulinemia, chronic inflammation, and elevated IGF-1 levels.
Metformin use has been associated with reduced breast cancer risk in some studies.
Standard mammography screening guidelines apply, with annual screening from age 40-50
depending on risk factors. Diabetic women should be especially vigilant about regular
screening given their elevated risk profile.

BONE HEALTH AND OSTEOPOROSIS

Type 1 diabetes is associated with significantly reduced bone density and a 6-7 fold
increased fracture risk. Type 2 diabetes paradoxically shows normal or increased bone
density but still carries a 1.3-1.7 fold increased fracture risk due to altered bone quality.
Thiazolidinediones (TZDs) decrease bone density and should be avoided in women with
osteoporosis risk. SGLT2 inhibitors may increase fracture risk in some populations.
Adequate calcium (1000-1200 mg daily) and vitamin D (800-1000 IU daily) supplementation,
weight-bearing exercise, and fall prevention strategies are essential.

PREGNANCY PLANNING WITH PRE-EXISTING DIABETES

Women with pre-existing diabetes should plan pregnancies with HbA1c below 6.5% before
conception. Folic acid supplementation (5 mg daily) should begin 3 months before conception.
Potentially teratogenic medications (ACE inhibitors, statins, certain oral hypoglycemics)
must be discontinued and replaced with pregnancy-safe alternatives. Tight glycemic control
during pregnancy reduces risk of congenital anomalies, macrosomia, and preeclampsia.

UROGENITAL HEALTH

Women with diabetes are more prone to urinary tract infections, vaginal yeast infections,
and sexual dysfunction. SGLT2 inhibitors increase genital infection risk. Good glycemic
control and proper hygiene reduce infection frequency. Sexual dysfunction affects up to
40% of diabetic women and should be addressed openly with healthcare providers.

CONCLUSION

Women with diabetes require gender-specific screening, treatment adjustments across
reproductive milestones, and proactive management of bone, cardiovascular, and cancer risk.
