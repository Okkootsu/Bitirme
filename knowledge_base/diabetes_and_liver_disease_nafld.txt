Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD/MASLD) and Diabetes
Sources: EASL-EASD-EASO Clinical Practice Guidelines 2024, ADA Standards of Care 2024, Hepatology, Journal of Hepatology

OVERVIEW AND NOMENCLATURE

- NAFLD (Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease) has been renamed to MASLD (Metabolic Dysfunction-Associated Steatotic Liver Disease) in 2023 by international consensus
- Defined as hepatic steatosis (≥5% fat) in the presence of at least one cardiometabolic risk factor, without significant alcohol consumption
- Spectrum: Simple steatosis → NASH (Non-Alcoholic Steatohepatitis) / MASH → Fibrosis → Cirrhosis → Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC)

EPIDEMIOLOGY

- Global prevalence: 25-30% of general population, 55-70% of people with Type 2 diabetes
- NASH prevalence in T2DM: 35-40%
- Advanced fibrosis (F3-F4) in T2DM with NAFLD: 15-20%
- NAFLD is now the leading cause of chronic liver disease worldwide
- In Turkey: NAFLD prevalence estimated at 30-40% in general population, higher in metabolic syndrome

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

The "Multiple-Hit Hypothesis":
1. Insulin resistance → increased hepatic de novo lipogenesis and impaired fatty acid oxidation → hepatic steatosis
2. Lipotoxicity → oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, endoplasmic reticulum stress
3. Inflammatory activation → Kupffer cell activation, cytokine release (TNF-α, IL-6)
4. Gut microbiome dysbiosis → increased intestinal permeability → portal endotoxemia
5. Genetic susceptibility → PNPLA3, TM6SF2 gene variants increase risk

Bidirectional relationship:
- Diabetes worsens NAFLD progression (hyperglycemia, hyperinsulinemia accelerate fibrosis)
- NAFLD worsens diabetes (hepatic insulin resistance, impaired glycogen storage, gluconeogenesis dysregulation)
- NAFLD independently increases cardiovascular disease risk by 1.5-2x

SCREENING AND DIAGNOSIS

Who to screen:
- All patients with T2DM should be assessed for NAFLD (ADA 2024)
- Patients with obesity, metabolic syndrome, elevated ALT

Non-invasive assessment:
- FIB-4 score: Age, AST, ALT, platelet count → stratifies fibrosis risk
  * FIB-4 <1.3: Low risk (no further workup)
  * FIB-4 1.3-2.67: Indeterminate (proceed to elastography)
  * FIB-4 >2.67: High risk (refer to hepatology)
- Transient elastography (FibroScan): Liver stiffness measurement
  * <8 kPa: No significant fibrosis
  * 8-12 kPa: Significant fibrosis possible
  * >12 kPa: Advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis likely
- NAFLD Fibrosis Score (NFS): Age, BMI, diabetes status, AST/ALT, platelet, albumin
- Liver biopsy: Gold standard but invasive; reserved for diagnostic uncertainty

Imaging:
- Ultrasound: First-line screening (≥20% steatosis detectable)
- MRI-PDFF (Proton Density Fat Fraction): Most accurate for steatosis quantification
- CT scan: Less sensitive than ultrasound for mild steatosis

TREATMENT

Lifestyle intervention (FIRST LINE for all):
- Weight loss of 7-10% improves steatosis, inflammation, and fibrosis
- >10% weight loss may reverse established fibrosis
- Mediterranean diet: Best evidence (reduces hepatic fat independent of weight loss)
- Exercise: 150-200 min/week moderate-intensity; reduces hepatic fat even without weight loss
- Coffee: 3-4 cups/day associated with reduced fibrosis and HCC risk

Pharmacotherapy for NASH:
- Pioglitazone: Only drug with RCT evidence for histological improvement in NASH (reduces inflammation and fibrosis)
  * Dose: 30-45 mg/day
  * Side effects: Weight gain, fluid retention, fracture risk
  * Benefit persists in non-diabetic NASH patients
- Vitamin E (800 IU/day): Improves NASH histology in non-diabetic patients; not recommended for diabetic NASH or cirrhosis
- GLP-1 receptor agonists: Semaglutide (LEAN trial with liraglutide, phase 3 with semaglutide) — reduces NASH and improves fibrosis
  * Semaglutide 2.4 mg weekly: NASH resolution in 59% vs 17% placebo
- SGLT2 inhibitors: Reduce hepatic fat content by 20-30%; additional cardiorenal benefits
- Resmetirom (Rezdiffra): First FDA-approved drug specifically for NASH with fibrosis (2024); thyroid hormone receptor beta agonist

Agents to AVOID in NAFLD/cirrhosis:
- Sulfonylureas in advanced cirrhosis (hypoglycemia risk due to impaired gluconeogenesis)
- Metformin: Safe in NAFLD; previously contraindicated in cirrhosis but now considered safe if eGFR adequate; does NOT improve NASH histology

HEPATOCELLULAR CARCINOMA RISK

- NAFLD-related HCC can occur without cirrhosis (10-20% of NAFLD-HCC cases)
- T2DM independently increases HCC risk by 2-3x
- Surveillance: Ultrasound ± AFP every 6 months in patients with cirrhosis
- Screening in non-cirrhotic NAFLD is not standardized but should be considered in high-risk patients

CARDIOVASCULAR DISEASE AND NAFLD

- Leading cause of death in NAFLD patients is cardiovascular disease (not liver disease)
- NAFLD is an independent CV risk factor even after adjusting for traditional risk factors
- Aggressive CV risk management recommended: Statins (safe in NAFLD, may improve liver histology), blood pressure control, antiplatelet therapy as indicated
- Statins are SAFE in NAFLD and should NOT be withheld due to mildly elevated transaminases
