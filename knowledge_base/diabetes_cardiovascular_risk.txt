Diabetes and Cardiovascular Disease
Sources: ADA Standards of Care 2024, ESC/EASD Guidelines 2023, Framingham Heart Study, UKPDS, EMPA-REG OUTCOME, LEADER Trial

OVERVIEW

Cardiovascular disease (CVD) is the leading cause of morbidity and mortality in people with diabetes. Adults with diabetes have a 2-4 fold increased risk of cardiovascular events compared to those without diabetes. Approximately 50-80% of deaths in people with diabetes are attributable to cardiovascular causes.

CARDIOVASCULAR RISK IN DIABETES

Macrovascular complications:
- Coronary artery disease (CAD): 2-4x increased risk
- Cerebrovascular disease (stroke): 2-3x increased risk
- Peripheral arterial disease (PAD): 4-8x increased risk
- Heart failure: 2-5x increased risk (independent of CAD)

Key statistics:
- People with T2DM without prior MI have similar CV risk to non-diabetic individuals with prior MI ("coronary heart disease equivalent")
- Women with diabetes lose their usual cardiovascular protection compared to men
- CVD risk begins during the prediabetes stage
- Every 1% increase in HbA1c is associated with approximately 18% increased risk of cardiovascular events (UKPDS)

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY OF DIABETIC CARDIOVASCULAR DISEASE

1. Accelerated atherosclerosis:
- Endothelial dysfunction (reduced nitric oxide bioavailability)
- Increased oxidative stress and inflammation
- Advanced Glycation End-products (AGEs) modify LDL and collagen
- Glycated LDL is more atherogenic and not recognized by normal LDL receptors
- Increased VLDL production and small dense LDL particles

2. Diabetic cardiomyopathy:
- Direct myocardial damage independent of coronary artery disease or hypertension
- Mechanisms: lipotoxicity, glucotoxicity, microvascular disease, fibrosis, autonomic dysfunction
- Leads to diastolic dysfunction initially, then systolic dysfunction

3. Prothrombotic state:
- Increased platelet aggregation and adhesion
- Elevated fibrinogen, PAI-1, and von Willebrand factor
- Decreased fibrinolysis
- Increased risk of thrombus formation

4. Diabetic dyslipidemia:
- Characteristic pattern: High triglycerides, low HDL-C, elevated small dense LDL
- Even with normal total LDL, the particle composition is more atherogenic
- VLDL overproduction driven by insulin resistance and hepatic steatosis

RISK FACTOR MANAGEMENT

1. Glycemic Control:
- HbA1c <7% reduces microvascular complications significantly
- Intensive glycemic control has modest macrovascular benefit (UKPDS legacy effect seen after 10+ years)
- Avoid aggressive glycemic targets in patients with established CVD (ACCORD trial: increased mortality with HbA1c <6.0% target)
- Prioritize medications with proven CV benefit: GLP-1 RAs, SGLT2 inhibitors

2. Blood Pressure Management:
- Target: <130/80 mmHg for most patients with diabetes (ADA 2024)
- First-line: ACE inhibitors or ARBs (preferred due to renoprotective effects)
- Additional agents: Calcium channel blockers, thiazide diuretics
- Beta-blockers: Use for specific indications (post-MI, heart failure, rate control)
- ACE/ARB combination contraindicated (ONTARGET trial)

3. Lipid Management:
- Statin therapy recommended for virtually all adults with diabetes:
  * Age 40-75 without ASCVD: Moderate-intensity statin (atorvastatin 10-20mg, rosuvastatin 5-10mg)
  * Age 40-75 with ASCVD: High-intensity statin (atorvastatin 40-80mg, rosuvastatin 20-40mg)
  * LDL target in very high risk: <55 mg/dL (ESC/EAS), <70 mg/dL (ADA)
- If LDL not at goal on max statin: Add ezetimibe, then consider PCSK9 inhibitor
- Triglycerides >500 mg/dL: Fibrate or icosapent ethyl to prevent pancreatitis
- Icosapent ethyl (EPA): REDUCE-IT trial showed 25% reduction in CV events in high-risk patients on statins with TG 150-499 mg/dL

4. Antiplatelet Therapy:
- Aspirin (75-162 mg/day): Recommended for secondary prevention in all patients with ASCVD
- Primary prevention: Consider aspirin in patients with high CV risk (10-year ASCVD risk >20%) and low bleeding risk
- Dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin + clopidogrel/ticagrelor): After acute coronary syndrome or PCI, typically for 12 months

5. Smoking Cessation:
- Smoking cessation is the single most impactful lifestyle modification for CV risk reduction
- Diabetes patients who smoke have 2-3x additional CV risk beyond diabetes alone
- Offer pharmacotherapy: Varenicline, bupropion, nicotine replacement therapy

HEART FAILURE AND DIABETES

- Heart failure affects approximately 22% of people with T2DM
- SGLT2 inhibitors are now standard of care for heart failure in diabetes:
  * Empagliflozin: EMPEROR-Reduced and EMPEROR-Preserved trials
  * Dapagliflozin: DAPA-HF trial
  * Benefits seen in both HFrEF (reduced ejection fraction) and HFpEF (preserved EF)
  * 25-30% reduction in heart failure hospitalization
- Avoid TZDs (pioglitazone, rosiglitazone) in heart failure
- Metformin is safe in stable heart failure (previously contraindicated, now reconsidered)
- Saxagliptin: Caution due to possible increased HF hospitalization (SAVOR-TIMI 53)

SCREENING RECOMMENDATIONS

- Annual assessment of cardiovascular risk factors (lipids, blood pressure, smoking status, weight)
- Resting ECG for patients with hypertension or suspected CVD
- Ankle-brachial index (ABI) for PAD symptoms
- Coronary artery calcium (CAC) scoring can refine risk in intermediate-risk patients
- Stress testing only if symptoms suggestive of CAD or high pre-test probability
