Diabetes and the Immune System: Immune Function, Vaccinations, Infection Prevention
Source: ADA Immunization Recommendations 2024; CDC Vaccination Guidelines; WHO Infectious Disease and Diabetes Reports

OVERVIEW

Diabetes significantly impairs immune function, increasing both the risk and severity of
infections. Individuals with diabetes are 2-3 times more likely to develop infections
compared to non-diabetic individuals, and infections account for approximately 10-15%
of diabetes-related deaths. Hyperglycemia directly suppresses immune cell function,
impairs wound healing, and promotes pathogen growth. In Turkey, where infectious diseases
and diabetes both present significant public health challenges, understanding the immune
consequences of diabetes is vital for prevention and management.

MECHANISMS OF IMMUNE DYSFUNCTION IN DIABETES

Chronic hyperglycemia disrupts immune function through multiple pathways. Neutrophil
dysfunction includes impaired chemotaxis (reduced ability to migrate to infection sites),
decreased phagocytosis (reduced ability to engulf and kill pathogens), and impaired
oxidative burst (reduced microbicidal capacity). These defects are proportional to the
degree of hyperglycemia and largely reversible with improved glucose control. T-cell and
B-cell function is also impaired, reducing adaptive immune responses. Complement system
activation is altered, weakening the innate immune defense. Additionally, glycosylation of
immunoglobulins reduces their effectiveness. The high-glucose environment directly
promotes growth of certain pathogens, including Candida species and Staphylococcus aureus.

COMMON INFECTIONS IN DIABETES

Several infections occur with increased frequency and severity in diabetic patients.
Respiratory infections including pneumonia, influenza, and COVID-19 are more severe in
diabetes, with 2-3 times higher hospitalization and mortality rates. Urinary tract infections
are 2-4 times more common in diabetic women due to glycosuria promoting bacterial growth
and neurogenic bladder from autonomic neuropathy. Skin and soft tissue infections including
cellulitis, abscesses, and diabetic foot infections are frequent and can progress rapidly.
Fungal infections particularly oral and genital candidiasis affect up to 30% of poorly
controlled diabetic patients. Tuberculosis risk is 2-3 times higher in diabetes, particularly
relevant in Turkey where TB remains a public health concern. Periodontal disease affects
up to 70% of diabetic individuals, creating a bidirectional relationship where gum disease
worsens glycemic control.

VACCINATION RECOMMENDATIONS

Vaccination is a critical preventive strategy for diabetic individuals. Influenza vaccine
should be given annually before each flu season. COVID-19 vaccination including boosters
is strongly recommended as diabetes increases severe COVID risk 2-3 fold. Pneumococcal
vaccination with PCV20 or PCV15 followed by PPSV23 is recommended for all diabetic
adults. Hepatitis B vaccination is recommended for unvaccinated diabetic adults aged
19-59 and considered for those over 60, as diabetes increases hepatitis B risk through
shared glucose monitoring equipment. Tdap (tetanus, diphtheria, pertussis) should follow
standard adult schedule with Td boosters every 10 years. Shingles vaccine (recombinant
zoster vaccine) is recommended for adults over 50 with diabetes. In Turkey, the national
immunization program covers influenza and pneumococcal vaccines for diabetic patients
at no cost through family health centers.

INFECTION PREVENTION STRATEGIES

Daily foot inspection and proper foot care prevent diabetic foot infections, the most common
cause of diabetes-related hospitalization. Skin care including moisturizing dry skin,
treating cuts promptly, and avoiding barefoot walking reduces skin infection risk. Oral
hygiene with regular dental check-ups every 6 months prevents periodontal disease.
Optimal glycemic control (HbA1c below 7-8%) is the single most effective measure to
improve immune function. Hand hygiene remains the simplest and most effective infection
prevention method. Food safety practices prevent gastrointestinal infections.

SURGICAL AND PROCEDURAL CONSIDERATIONS

Diabetic patients have 2-5 times higher surgical site infection rates. Perioperative glucose
management with target 140-180 mg/dL significantly reduces infection risk. Prophylactic
antibiotics may be extended for diabetic patients undergoing certain procedures. Wound
healing is delayed in diabetes, requiring careful postoperative monitoring.

COVID-19 AND DIABETES SPECIFIC CONSIDERATIONS

The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted the vulnerability of diabetic individuals to infectious
diseases. Diabetic patients with COVID-19 have 2-3 times higher ICU admission rates,
higher ventilation requirements, and 2-4 times higher mortality risk. Poorly controlled
diabetes (HbA1c above 9%) further increases these risks. New-onset diabetes has been
reported after COVID-19 infection, likely due to viral damage to pancreatic beta cells.

CONCLUSION

The immune dysfunction associated with diabetes is clinically significant but largely
manageable through glycemic optimization, appropriate vaccination, infection prevention
practices, and prompt treatment of infections when they occur.
