Thyroid Disorders and Diabetes Comorbidity
Sources: Endocrine Reviews, ADA Standards of Care 2024, European Thyroid Association (ETA) Guidelines, Thyroid Journal

OVERVIEW

Thyroid disorders and diabetes frequently coexist. The association is strongest between Type 1 diabetes and autoimmune thyroid disease, but thyroid dysfunction also significantly affects glycemic control in Type 2 diabetes. Thyroid function testing is an important component of comprehensive diabetes care.

EPIDEMIOLOGY

Type 1 Diabetes and Thyroid Disease:
- Autoimmune thyroid disease (AITD) is the most common autoimmune comorbidity in T1DM
- Prevalence of thyroid autoantibodies (anti-TPO, anti-Tg) in T1DM: 15-30%
- Clinical hypothyroidism in T1DM: 5-10% (vs 2-5% in general population)
- Hyperthyroidism (Graves' disease) in T1DM: 1-2%
- Shared genetic susceptibility: HLA-DR3, HLA-DR4, CTLA-4, PTPN22 gene variants
- Autoimmune polyglandular syndrome (APS) Type 2: T1DM + Hashimoto's + Addison's disease

Type 2 Diabetes and Thyroid Disease:
- Subclinical hypothyroidism prevalence in T2DM: 10-15%
- Clinical hypothyroidism: 5-8%
- Thyroid nodules: More common in metabolic syndrome and T2DM (insulin and IGF-1 as thyroid growth factors)
- Thyroid cancer: Slightly increased risk in T2DM (1.2-1.5x)

HYPOTHYROIDISM AND DIABETES

Effects on glucose metabolism:
- Reduced glucose absorption from gut
- Decreased hepatic glucose output
- Reduced insulin clearance → relative hyperinsulinemia
- Increased insulin resistance (especially in overt hypothyroidism)
- Net effect: Variable — may cause hypoglycemia (reduced glucose production) or hyperglycemia (insulin resistance)
- HbA1c may be falsely elevated in hypothyroidism (reduced red blood cell turnover)

Clinical impact:
- Worsened lipid profile: Elevated LDL, total cholesterol, triglycerides
- Increased cardiovascular risk (already elevated in diabetes)
- Weight gain and difficulty losing weight
- Fatigue, cold intolerance, constipation overlap with diabetes symptoms

Treatment:
- Levothyroxine replacement (standard dose: 1.6 mcg/kg/day)
- Start low in elderly and those with cardiac disease (25-50 mcg/day)
- TSH target: 0.5-4.0 mU/L for most adults; 1.0-3.0 for those on treatment
- Monitor glycemic control after starting levothyroxine (may need diabetes medication adjustment)
- Metformin may lower TSH levels (mechanism unclear); recheck TSH after metformin initiation/discontinuation

HYPERTHYROIDISM AND DIABETES

Effects on glucose metabolism:
- Increased hepatic glucose production (gluconeogenesis, glycogenolysis)
- Increased intestinal glucose absorption
- Accelerated insulin degradation
- Increased peripheral glucose utilization but net hyperglycemia
- Can unmask latent diabetes or worsen existing diabetes
- May precipitate DKA in T1DM

Clinical impact:
- Significant worsening of glycemic control
- Weight loss despite increased appetite
- Increased insulin requirements (may need 50-100% dose increase)
- Tachycardia, tremor, anxiety (overlap with hypoglycemia symptoms — important distinction)
- Graves' ophthalmopathy may coexist with diabetic retinopathy

Treatment:
- Antithyroid drugs (methimazole, propylthiouracil)
- Radioactive iodine therapy
- Surgery (thyroidectomy)
- After treatment: Hypothyroidism is common; glucose control may improve significantly
- Adjust diabetes medications as thyroid function normalizes

SUBCLINICAL THYROID DYSFUNCTION

Subclinical hypothyroidism (elevated TSH, normal free T4):
- Common in diabetes (10-15%)
- May worsen insulin resistance and lipid profile
- Treatment: Consider if TSH >10 mU/L, or TSH 5-10 with symptoms or positive TPO antibodies
- Monitor every 6-12 months if not treating

Subclinical hyperthyroidism (suppressed TSH, normal free T4/T3):
- Less common (1-2%)
- Associated with atrial fibrillation risk and bone loss
- Treatment based on degree of TSH suppression and symptoms

SCREENING RECOMMENDATIONS

ADA 2024 recommendations:
- Type 1 Diabetes:
  * Screen TSH at diagnosis
  * Repeat TSH annually or with symptoms
  * Consider anti-TPO antibodies at diagnosis to assess future risk
  * Screen for other autoimmune conditions (celiac disease, Addison's)

- Type 2 Diabetes:
  * No routine screening recommended for all T2DM patients
  * Check TSH if symptoms of thyroid dysfunction, dyslipidemia not responding to treatment, unexplained glycemic variability, or goiter on exam
  * Consider screening in women with T2DM (higher prevalence)

SICK EUTHYROID SYNDROME (Non-Thyroidal Illness)

- Abnormal thyroid function tests during acute illness WITHOUT true thyroid disease
- Pattern: Low T3, low or normal T4, low or normal TSH
- Common in hospitalized patients with diabetes (DKA, HHS, severe infections)
- No treatment with thyroid hormone needed (resolves with recovery)
- Do NOT diagnose thyroid disease based on tests during acute illness; recheck 6-8 weeks after recovery

MEDICATION INTERACTIONS

- Metformin: May lower TSH in patients with hypothyroidism; mechanism debated (central effect vs peripheral)
- Levothyroxine absorption: Reduced by calcium, iron, PPIs (common in diabetes patients); take on empty stomach, 30-60 minutes before breakfast
- GLP-1 RAs: Liraglutide and semaglutide carry a thyroid C-cell tumor warning based on rodent studies; no human signal confirmed; contraindicated in personal/family history of medullary thyroid cancer (MTC) or MEN2
- Pioglitazone: May increase thyroid-stimulating antibodies in some studies (clinical significance unclear)
