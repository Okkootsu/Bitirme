Diabetic Emergencies: Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) and Hyperglycemic Hyperosmolar State (HHS)
Sources: ADA Consensus Report 2024, Kitabchi AE et al. Diabetes Care, Joint British Diabetes Societies (JBDS) Guidelines

DIABETIC KETOACIDOSIS (DKA)

Definition: Acute, life-threatening complication characterized by hyperglycemia, metabolic acidosis, and ketosis due to absolute or relative insulin deficiency.

Epidemiology:
- Annual incidence: 4-8 per 1,000 diabetes patients
- Mortality: 1-5% in developed countries; up to 30% in developing countries
- Leading cause of death in children and young adults with T1DM
- 25-40% of T1DM patients present with DKA at initial diagnosis
- Can occur in T2DM during severe physiological stress

Pathophysiology:
1. Insulin deficiency + counterregulatory hormone excess (glucagon, cortisol, catecholamines, growth hormone)
2. Hepatic gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis → hyperglycemia
3. Lipolysis → free fatty acids → hepatic ketogenesis → acetoacetate, β-hydroxybutyrate, acetone
4. Ketone accumulation → metabolic acidosis (anion gap acidosis)
5. Osmotic diuresis → dehydration, electrolyte loss (Na⁺, K⁺, PO₄, Mg²⁺)

Diagnostic Criteria (ADA):
- Blood glucose >250 mg/dL (may be lower in euglycemic DKA)
- Arterial pH <7.30
- Serum bicarbonate <18 mEq/L
- Anion gap >12
- Ketonemia (serum β-hydroxybutyrate >3.0 mmol/L) or ketonuria

Severity Classification:
- Mild: pH 7.25-7.30, bicarbonate 15-18, alert mental status
- Moderate: pH 7.00-7.24, bicarbonate 10-14, alert/drowsy
- Severe: pH <7.00, bicarbonate <10, stupor/coma

Precipitating Factors:
- Infection (most common, 30-40%): Pneumonia, UTI, sepsis
- Insulin omission or inadequate dosing (20-25%)
- New-onset diabetes (15-20%)
- Myocardial infarction, stroke, pancreatitis
- Medications: Corticosteroids, SGLT2 inhibitors, atypical antipsychotics
- Substance abuse (cocaine, alcohol)
- Pregnancy

Treatment Protocol (Kitabchi):

1. IV Fluid Resuscitation:
   - Start: 0.9% NaCl at 15-20 mL/kg/hr (1-1.5 L in first hour)
   - Then: 0.9% NaCl at 250-500 mL/hr (adjust based on hydration status)
   - When glucose reaches 200 mg/dL: Switch to D5-0.45% NaCl at 150-250 mL/hr
   - Total deficit typically 5-7 liters

2. Insulin Therapy:
   - Regular insulin IV bolus: 0.1 U/kg, then continuous infusion 0.1 U/kg/hr
   - Alternative: 0.14 U/kg/hr without bolus
   - Target glucose decrease: 50-75 mg/dL per hour
   - When glucose reaches 200 mg/dL: Reduce insulin to 0.02-0.05 U/kg/hr
   - Continue IV insulin until DKA resolution criteria met

3. Potassium Replacement:
   - If K⁺ <3.3 mEq/L: Hold insulin, give 20-40 mEq/hr until K⁺ >3.3
   - If K⁺ 3.3-5.3 mEq/L: Add 20-30 mEq/L to each liter of IV fluid
   - If K⁺ >5.3 mEq/L: Do not supplement; recheck every 2 hours
   - Monitor K⁺ every 1-2 hours

4. Bicarbonate: Only if pH <6.9 (100 mmol NaHCO₃ in 400 mL water with 20 mEq KCl over 2 hours)

5. Phosphate: Replace if <1.0 mg/dL or if cardiac/respiratory dysfunction

Resolution Criteria:
- Glucose <200 mg/dL AND at least two of:
  * Serum bicarbonate ≥15 mEq/L
  * Venous pH >7.3
  * Anion gap ≤12
- Transition to subcutaneous insulin: Give SC insulin 1-2 hours BEFORE stopping IV insulin
- Patient must be eating and drinking

Euglycemic DKA:
- DKA with blood glucose <250 mg/dL
- Increasingly recognized with SGLT2 inhibitor use
- SGLT2i mechanism: Glucosuria masks hyperglycemia while ketogenesis continues
- Management: Same as standard DKA but with earlier dextrose-containing fluids
- Prevention: Stop SGLT2i 3-4 days before surgery, during prolonged fasting, or acute illness

HYPERGLYCEMIC HYPEROSMOLAR STATE (HHS)

Definition: Severe hyperglycemia with hyperosmolality and dehydration without significant ketoacidosis. More common in T2DM.

Diagnostic Criteria:
- Blood glucose >600 mg/dL
- Serum osmolality >320 mOsm/kg
- pH >7.30 (minimal acidosis)
- Serum bicarbonate >18 mEq/L
- Absent to mild ketonemia
- Altered mental status (confusion, lethargy, coma)

Key Differences from DKA:
- More gradual onset (days to weeks vs hours)
- More severe dehydration (average fluid deficit 8-12 liters)
- Higher mortality (10-20% vs 1-5% for DKA)
- More common in elderly, often with impaired thirst mechanism
- Sufficient insulin to prevent ketosis but insufficient to control glucose

Precipitating Factors:
- Infection (40-60%): Most common precipitant
- Poor fluid intake (especially in elderly, nursing home residents)
- Medications: Diuretics, corticosteroids, phenytoin
- New-onset T2DM
- Renal failure, stroke, MI

Treatment:
- Aggressive IV fluid replacement (0.9% NaCl, then 0.45% NaCl when Na⁺ corrected)
- Careful insulin therapy (lower doses than DKA; avoid rapid glucose correction)
- Target glucose decrease: 50-70 mg/dL per hour (rapid correction → cerebral edema risk)
- Electrolyte replacement (K⁺, PO₄, Mg²⁺)
- Thromboprophylaxis (high VTE risk due to hyperosmolality and dehydration)
- Treat underlying cause

PREVENTION STRATEGIES

- Sick day education for all diabetes patients
- Never stop insulin during illness (even if not eating)
- Ensure access to insulin and glucose monitoring supplies
- Ketone monitoring for T1DM patients during illness
- Hold SGLT2i during acute illness
- Early medical contact when glucose >300 mg/dL or ketones positive
- Diabetes identification (medical alert bracelet/card)
