Diabetes and Men's Health: Testosterone, Erectile Dysfunction, Cardiovascular Risk
Source: ADA Standards of Care 2024; Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guidelines; European Association of Urology

OVERVIEW

Diabetes disproportionately affects men's health through multiple pathways that are often
underdiagnosed and undertreated. Men with Type 2 diabetes have a 2-3 times higher risk of
testosterone deficiency compared to non-diabetic men. Erectile dysfunction affects 35-75%
of diabetic men, appearing 10-15 years earlier than in the general population. Cardiovascular
disease remains the leading cause of death among diabetic men. Despite these significant
risks, men are less likely to seek medical attention and adhere to diabetes management plans.

TESTOSTERONE AND DIABETES

Low testosterone (hypogonadism) and Type 2 diabetes share a bidirectional relationship.
Low testosterone promotes insulin resistance, visceral fat accumulation, and metabolic
syndrome, while diabetes and obesity suppress testosterone production. Approximately
25-40% of men with Type 2 diabetes have low testosterone levels. Symptoms include
fatigue, decreased libido, reduced muscle mass, increased body fat, mood changes, and
poor concentration. Screening is recommended for men with Type 2 diabetes who present
with sexual dysfunction, unexplained fatigue, or loss of muscle mass. Total testosterone
below 300 ng/dL on two morning samples confirms diagnosis. Testosterone replacement
therapy may be considered but must weigh benefits against cardiovascular risks, especially
in men over 65 or those with existing heart disease.

ERECTILE DYSFUNCTION

Erectile dysfunction (ED) is a common and distressing complication of diabetes affecting
quality of life and mental health. The pathophysiology involves endothelial dysfunction,
neuropathy, hormonal imbalance, and psychological factors. ED can also be an early marker
of cardiovascular disease and should prompt cardiac evaluation. Treatment options include
optimizing glycemic control (which may improve mild ED), PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil,
tadalafil) as first-line therapy, vacuum erection devices, intracavernosal injections, and
penile prosthesis as last resort. Drug interactions must be considered: PDE5 inhibitors
are contraindicated with nitrates. Psychological counseling and couples therapy can
address the emotional impact.

CARDIOVASCULAR RISK

Men with diabetes have a 2-4 times higher risk of cardiovascular disease compared to
non-diabetic men. Key risk factors include hypertension (present in 70-80% of diabetic
men), dyslipidemia (elevated LDL, low HDL, high triglycerides), smoking, abdominal obesity,
and physical inactivity. Aggressive risk factor management is essential: blood pressure
target below 130/80 mmHg, statin therapy for most men over 40, aspirin for secondary
prevention, smoking cessation, and regular physical activity. SGLT2 inhibitors and GLP-1
receptor agonists have shown cardiovascular benefits beyond glucose lowering and should
be preferred in high-risk patients.

SCREENING RECOMMENDATIONS FOR MEN

Annual screening should include HbA1c and fasting glucose, lipid panel, kidney function
(eGFR, urine albumin-creatinine ratio), blood pressure measurement, foot examination,
eye examination, and assessment of sexual function. Prostate cancer screening discussions
should also occur, particularly as testosterone therapy may influence prostate tissue.

MENTAL HEALTH IN DIABETIC MEN

Men are less likely to report depression symptoms or seek psychological help. Depression
prevalence in diabetic men is approximately 15-20%, significantly higher than the general
male population. Untreated depression worsens glycemic control, reduces treatment
adherence, and increases cardiovascular risk. Healthcare providers should proactively
screen for depression using validated tools.

PRACTICAL ADVICE

Regular exercise (150 minutes per week moderate intensity), maintaining a healthy weight
(BMI below 25), limiting alcohol consumption, quitting smoking, managing stress, and
maintaining regular health check-ups form the foundation of disease management for men
with diabetes. Open communication with healthcare providers about sensitive topics like
sexual dysfunction is essential for comprehensive care.

CONCLUSION

Men with diabetes face unique health challenges that require targeted screening, early
intervention, and holistic management addressing physical, sexual, and mental health.
