Diabetes and Cultural Food Practices: Religious Fasting, Celebrations, Food Traditions
Source: IDF-DAR Fasting Guidelines 2021; ADA Cultural Competence in Diabetes Care; WHO Nutrition Reports

OVERVIEW

Food is deeply intertwined with culture, religion, and social identity. For individuals with
diabetes, navigating cultural food practices requires balancing health needs with traditions
that are central to their lives. Across the world, religious fasting periods, cultural
celebrations, and traditional dietary patterns all affect diabetes management. Rather than
asking patients to abandon their cultural practices, healthcare providers should work
collaboratively to adapt diabetes management within cultural contexts. This culturally
sensitive approach improves adherence, satisfaction, and outcomes.

RAMADAN FASTING AND DIABETES

Ramadan fasting represents the most studied intersection of religious practice and diabetes
management. Approximately 50 million Muslims with diabetes fast during Ramadan worldwide.
In Turkey, an estimated 3-4 million diabetic individuals observe the fast. The IDF-DAR
guidelines categorize risk levels: very high risk patients (Type 1, recent DKA, pregnancy)
should not fast; high risk patients (poorly controlled Type 2, renal disease) should avoid
fasting; moderate and low risk patients may fast with medical supervision. Key management
strategies include pre-Ramadan medical assessment 6-8 weeks before, medication timing
adjustment (shifting doses to suhoor and iftar), blood glucose monitoring during fasting
(which does not break the fast per religious authority consensus), breaking the fast
immediately if blood glucose drops below 70 mg/dL or rises above 300 mg/dL, and balanced
suhoor and iftar meals emphasizing complex carbohydrates, protein, and healthy fats.

ORTHODOX CHRISTIAN FASTING

Orthodox Christian fasting periods total approximately 180 days per year and typically
restrict animal products. For diabetic individuals, prolonged vegan-style fasting can affect
protein intake, vitamin B12 levels, and carbohydrate-fat balance. Legumes, nuts, and seeds
become important protein sources. Increased carbohydrate intake from grains during fasting
may raise blood glucose. Planning with a dietitian before fasting periods is advisable.

YOM KIPPUR AND JEWISH FASTING

Yom Kippur involves a 25-hour complete fast including water. For diabetic patients,
especially those on insulin, this poses significant hypoglycemia risk. Religious authorities
generally exempt those with health conditions from fasting. If a diabetic person chooses
to fast, close medical supervision, medication adjustment, and clear criteria for breaking
the fast are essential.

TURKISH CULTURAL CELEBRATIONS AND FOOD

Turkish cultural life revolves around food-centric gatherings. Bayram celebrations feature
high-sugar traditional sweets like baklava and lokum. Wedding and engagement ceremonies
offer extensive buffets. Funeral gatherings (taziye) traditionally serve sweet helva. Tea
culture involves sugar-laden tea multiple times daily. Strategies for diabetic individuals
include taking small portions of traditional sweets rather than avoiding them entirely,
choosing lower-sugar alternatives when available, eating a balanced meal before attending
celebrations, offering to bring a diabetes-friendly dish to share, and adjusting medication
timing around celebration meals.

MEDITERRANEAN DIET AND TURKISH CUISINE

Traditional Turkish cuisine shares many elements with the Mediterranean diet, which is
strongly associated with improved diabetes outcomes and 30% reduced cardiovascular risk.
Beneficial elements include olive oil as the primary fat source, abundant vegetable dishes
(zeytinyagli), legume-based meals (mercimek, fasulye), yogurt and fermented dairy, nuts
and seeds. Less favorable aspects include white bread consumption (Turkey has one of the
highest per-capita bread consumption rates globally), rice pilav as a staple, sugary
beverages and fruit juices, and large portion sizes.

SOUTH ASIAN FOOD TRADITIONS

South Asian populations have among the highest global diabetes rates. Traditional dietary
patterns include high refined carbohydrate intake (white rice, naan), ghee and coconut oil
use, sweet preparations for celebrations, and large evening meals. Adaptations include
replacing white rice with brown rice or cauliflower rice, using less ghee, increasing
vegetable portions, and distributing calories more evenly across meals.

PRACTICAL CULTURAL ADAPTATION STRATEGIES

Healthcare providers should take a food history that includes cultural and religious practices,
involve family members who prepare meals in nutrition education, provide culturally specific
meal plans and recipes, respect food traditions while suggesting healthier modifications,
and recognize that food restriction can cause significant psychological distress.

CONCLUSION

Culturally competent diabetes care acknowledges that food carries profound personal and
social meaning. Working within rather than against cultural food practices leads to
sustainable diabetes management and improved quality of life.
