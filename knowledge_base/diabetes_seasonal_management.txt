Seasonal Diabetes Management
Source: American Diabetes Association; Diabetes UK; Turkish Endocrinology and Metabolism Society

SUMMER AND HEAT MANAGEMENT

High temperatures affect diabetes management in multiple ways. Heat causes blood vessels to dilate, which can speed insulin absorption and increase hypoglycemia risk. Dehydration from sweating raises blood glucose concentration. Heat can damage insulin and testing supplies.

Summer precautions:
- Insulin storage: Keep insulin below 30C. Never leave in a car or direct sunlight. Use insulated cooling cases when traveling. Insulin exposed to heat loses potency without visible changes.
- Hydration: Drink at least 2-3 liters of water daily. Avoid sugary drinks and excess caffeine. Monitor for dehydration signs: dark urine, dizziness, dry mouth.
- Blood sugar monitoring: Test more frequently in extreme heat. CGM sensors may be less accurate with heavy sweating. Keep test strips away from heat and humidity.
- Exercise timing: Avoid outdoor exercise during peak heat (11am-4pm). Early morning or evening is ideal. Wear breathable, light-colored clothing. Carry fast-acting glucose.
- Foot care: Never walk barefoot on hot surfaces (sand, pavement). Diabetic neuropathy means you may not feel burns. Wear protective water shoes at beach and pool.

WINTER AND COLD WEATHER

Cold weather presents its own challenges for diabetes management. Blood sugar tends to run higher in winter due to reduced activity, holiday eating, and illness season.

Winter considerations:
- Illness and infections: Flu and respiratory infections raise blood sugar significantly. Get annual flu vaccine and COVID boosters. Have a sick day plan ready: check blood sugar every 2-4 hours, stay hydrated, know when to call doctor.
- Reduced physical activity: Shorter days and cold weather reduce outdoor exercise. Plan indoor alternatives: home workouts, mall walking, gym, swimming in heated pools.
- Holiday eating: Festive foods are often high in carbohydrates and sugar. Plan portions ahead. Do not skip meals to "save" calories for later. Monitor blood sugar before and 2 hours after holiday meals.
- Insulin and supply storage: Do not let insulin freeze (damaged permanently). Keep supplies at room temperature. In very cold weather, carry insulin close to body under clothing.

SPRING ALLERGY SEASON

Allergies and diabetes:
- Some allergy medications (decongestants with pseudoephedrine) can raise blood sugar
- Steroid nasal sprays generally have minimal effect on glucose
- Oral corticosteroids (prednisone) for severe allergies can dramatically increase blood sugar
- Inform your doctor about diabetes before starting any allergy treatment

RAMADAN AND RELIGIOUS FASTING

Approximately 50 million Muslims with diabetes fast during Ramadan worldwide. Fasting poses risks of hypoglycemia during the day and hyperglycemia after iftar meals.

Key recommendations from IDF-DAR guidelines:
- Risk assessment before Ramadan: Very high risk patients should not fast
- Medication timing adjustment needed (consult doctor 6-8 weeks before Ramadan)
- Suhoor: Eat complex carbohydrates for slow release. Include protein and healthy fats.
- Iftar: Break fast with water and dates, then eat a balanced meal. Avoid overeating.
- Blood sugar monitoring: Check at least 3-4 times daily. Breaking fast is recommended if glucose drops below 70 mg/dL or exceeds 300 mg/dL.

SEASONAL FOOD ADAPTATION

- Summer fruits: Watermelon, melon have high GI. Eat in small portions with protein.
- Winter comfort foods: Soups can be diabetes-friendly (lentil, vegetable). Avoid cream-based soups.
- Seasonal vegetables: Take advantage of fresh produce in each season. Aim for variety and color.
