ADA Standards of Care in Diabetes - 2024 Summary
Source: American Diabetes Association, Standards of Care in Diabetes — 2024 (Diabetes Care, Volume 47, Supplement 1)

CLASSIFICATION AND DIAGNOSIS

Diagnostic criteria for diabetes (any one of the following):
- Fasting Plasma Glucose (FPG) ≥126 mg/dL (7.0 mmol/L) — fasting = no caloric intake for ≥8 hours
- 2-hour plasma glucose ≥200 mg/dL (11.1 mmol/L) during 75g OGTT
- HbA1c ≥6.5% (48 mmol/mol)
- Random plasma glucose ≥200 mg/dL (11.1 mmol/L) with classic hyperglycemic symptoms
- In the absence of unequivocal hyperglycemia, diagnosis requires two abnormal test results from the same sample or two separate test samples

Prediabetes criteria:
- IFG: FPG 100-125 mg/dL (5.6-6.9 mmol/L)
- IGT: 2-hour OGTT 140-199 mg/dL (7.8-11.0 mmol/L)
- HbA1c: 5.7-6.4% (39-47 mmol/mol)

GLYCEMIC TARGETS

Adults (non-pregnant):
- HbA1c: <7.0% for most adults
- Pre-meal glucose: 80-130 mg/dL
- Post-meal glucose (1-2 hours after start of meal): <180 mg/dL
- CGM Time in Range (70-180 mg/dL): >70%

More stringent targets (HbA1c <6.5%):
- If achievable without significant hypoglycemia
- Short duration of diabetes, no significant CVD, younger age

Less stringent targets (HbA1c <8.0% or <8.5%):
- History of severe hypoglycemia
- Limited life expectancy
- Advanced complications
- Extensive comorbidities
- Long-standing diabetes with difficulty achieving tighter control

COMPREHENSIVE MEDICAL EVALUATION

Initial evaluation should include:
- Complete medical history (diabetes history, family history, complications, medications)
- Physical examination (BMI, blood pressure, foot exam, fundoscopic exam)
- Laboratory tests: HbA1c, fasting lipid panel, liver function, UACR, serum creatinine/eGFR, TSH (in T1DM)
- Referrals: Eye exam, dental exam, diabetes education, nutrition counseling, mental health screening

LIFESTYLE MANAGEMENT

Medical Nutrition Therapy (MNT):
- Individualized nutrition plan by registered dietitian
- No single ideal macronutrient distribution; focus on overall dietary pattern quality
- Reduce added sugars and refined grains
- Emphasize non-starchy vegetables, whole grains, legumes, nuts, seeds
- Mediterranean, DASH, and plant-based dietary patterns recommended
- Carbohydrate counting or consistent carbohydrate intake for insulin users

Physical Activity:
- 150+ min/week moderate-to-vigorous aerobic activity (spread over ≥3 days, no more than 2 consecutive days without)
- 2-3 sessions/week resistance training on non-consecutive days
- Reduce prolonged sedentary time (interrupt every 30 minutes)
- Pre-exercise glucose: 100-250 mg/dL ideal for most activities
- Flexibility and balance exercises for older adults (fall prevention)

Weight Management:
- 5% weight loss improves glycemic control, lipids, blood pressure
- 7-10% weight loss provides additional benefit
- 15%+ weight loss may lead to diabetes remission in some T2DM patients
- Metabolic surgery (bariatric surgery) recommended for BMI ≥40 or BMI ≥35 with uncontrolled diabetes

Smoking Cessation:
- Advise all patients not to smoke or use tobacco products
- Include e-cigarettes in smoking cessation counseling
- Offer pharmacotherapy and behavioral support

PHARMACOLOGICAL THERAPY (T2DM)

First-line: Metformin + lifestyle modification

Second-line selection based on compelling indications:
- Established ASCVD or high CV risk → GLP-1 RA or SGLT2i with proven CV benefit
- Heart failure (HFrEF or HFpEF) → SGLT2i
- CKD (albuminuria or eGFR 20-60) → SGLT2i, and/or finerenone (if albuminuria), GLP-1 RA
- Need to minimize hypoglycemia → GLP-1 RA, SGLT2i, DPP-4i, TZD
- Need to minimize weight gain or promote weight loss → GLP-1 RA > SGLT2i > DPP-4i
- Cost is a major issue → Sulfonylurea, TZD, insulin

Insulin initiation:
- Consider early if HbA1c >10%, blood glucose >300 mg/dL, or symptoms of hyperglycemia
- Start with basal insulin (typically 10 units or 0.1-0.2 U/kg/day)
- Titrate to fasting glucose target (usually increase by 2 units every 3 days)
- Add prandial insulin if needed for postprandial control

CARDIOVASCULAR RISK MANAGEMENT

Blood Pressure:
- Target: <130/80 mmHg
- First-line: ACE inhibitor or ARB (especially if albuminuria present)
- Lifestyle: DASH diet, sodium restriction (<2300 mg/day), weight loss, exercise, moderate alcohol

Lipids:
- Moderate-intensity statin: All patients 40-75 without ASCVD
- High-intensity statin: Patients with ASCVD, or age 40-75 with risk factors
- Add ezetimibe if LDL not at goal, then consider PCSK9 inhibitor
- Triglycerides ≥500: Fibrate or omega-3 for pancreatitis prevention

Antiplatelet:
- Aspirin 75-162 mg: Secondary prevention (established ASCVD)
- Primary prevention: Consider if high CV risk and low bleeding risk

MICROVASCULAR COMPLICATIONS SCREENING SCHEDULE

Diabetic Retinopathy:
- T1DM: Within 5 years of diagnosis, then annually
- T2DM: At diagnosis, then annually
- Every 2 years acceptable if no retinopathy and good glycemic control

Diabetic Kidney Disease:
- Annual UACR and eGFR for all patients
- T1DM: Start 5 years after diagnosis
- T2DM: Start at diagnosis

Diabetic Neuropathy:
- T1DM: 5 years after diagnosis, then annually
- T2DM: At diagnosis, then annually
- Annual comprehensive foot exam for all

SPECIAL POPULATIONS

Older Adults (≥65 years):
- Individualize glycemic targets based on functional status and comorbidities
- Healthy: HbA1c <7.0-7.5%
- Complex/intermediate: HbA1c <8.0%
- Very complex/poor health: HbA1c <8.5%, avoid symptomatic hyperglycemia
- Simplify treatment regimens, avoid hypoglycemia
- Screen for cognitive impairment, depression, falls

Children and Adolescents:
- T1DM: HbA1c <7.0% (previously <7.5%, updated 2024)
- T2DM in youth: Metformin first-line, liraglutide approved for ages ≥10
- Screen for complications starting 5 years after T1DM diagnosis or at T2DM diagnosis
- Psychosocial support critical during adolescence

Pregnancy:
- Pre-existing diabetes: Optimize HbA1c <6.5% before conception
- Insulin is the preferred treatment during pregnancy
- Target glucose: Fasting <95, 1h post-meal <140, 2h post-meal <120 mg/dL

DIABETES SELF-MANAGEMENT EDUCATION AND SUPPORT (DSMES)

- All people with diabetes should receive DSMES at diagnosis, annually, when complications develop, and during transitions of care
- Four critical time points: At diagnosis, annually, when new complications arise, when transitions in care occur
- Topics: Disease process, nutrition, physical activity, medication management, monitoring, prevention of complications, problem solving, psychosocial adjustment
- Delivered by certified diabetes educators (CDCES)
- Associated with improved HbA1c (0.5-1% reduction), reduced hospitalizations, improved quality of life
