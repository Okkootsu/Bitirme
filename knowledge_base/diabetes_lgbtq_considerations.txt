Diabetes Care for LGBTQ+ Individuals: Hormone Therapy, Healthcare Barriers, Inclusive Care
Source: ADA Standards of Care 2024; Endocrine Society Transgender Guidelines; WHO Health Equity Reports

OVERVIEW

LGBTQ+ individuals with diabetes face unique healthcare challenges including hormone
therapy interactions with glucose metabolism, higher rates of mental health conditions,
healthcare avoidance due to discrimination, and lack of provider training in LGBTQ+
competent care. Studies indicate that LGBTQ+ individuals have a 1.5-2 times higher risk
of diabetes compared to the general population, partly attributed to minority stress,
higher rates of obesity in certain subgroups, and disparities in healthcare access.
Understanding these intersections is essential for providing equitable diabetes care.

HORMONE THERAPY AND GLUCOSE METABOLISM

Gender-affirming hormone therapy (GAHT) can significantly affect glucose metabolism and
diabetes management. Estrogen therapy in transgender women may increase insulin
sensitivity but also raises triglycerides and thromboembolism risk. Testosterone therapy
in transgender men can promote insulin resistance, increase visceral adiposity, and
potentially worsen glycemic control. Monitoring recommendations include baseline metabolic
panel before starting GAHT, HbA1c every 3 months during the first year, lipid panel every
6 months, and ongoing assessment of cardiovascular risk factors. Diabetes medication
adjustments may be necessary when starting or changing GAHT. Metformin dosing may
need reevaluation as body composition changes with hormone therapy.

SPECIFIC MEDICATION INTERACTIONS

SGLT2 inhibitors require careful consideration in transgender individuals, particularly those
who have undergone genital surgery, due to altered urogenital anatomy and infection risk
patterns. Spironolactone, commonly used as an anti-androgen, can affect potassium levels
and interact with ACE inhibitors frequently prescribed for diabetic nephropathy. Cyproterone
acetate used in some countries may affect liver function and interact with diabetes
medications metabolized hepatically. GLP-1 receptor agonists are generally safe with
GAHT but weight loss effects should be monitored in individuals already underweight.

HEALTHCARE BARRIERS

Research consistently shows that LGBTQ+ individuals face significant healthcare barriers.
Approximately 30% of transgender individuals report being refused medical care or
experiencing discrimination in healthcare settings. Fear of discrimination leads to
delayed care, missed screenings, and avoidance of preventive services. In Turkey and
many other countries, limited provider education on LGBTQ+ health creates additional gaps.
Consequences include later diabetes diagnosis, poorer glycemic control, higher
complication rates, and reduced engagement with healthcare systems.

MENTAL HEALTH CONSIDERATIONS

LGBTQ+ individuals with diabetes experience compounded minority stress from both their
identity and chronic illness. Depression rates are 2-3 times higher than in heterosexual
cisgender individuals with diabetes. Eating disorders are more prevalent, particularly in
gay and bisexual men and transgender individuals, complicating nutritional management of
diabetes. Substance use rates are higher, affecting medication adherence and glycemic
control. Screening for mental health conditions should be routine and conducted using
affirming language and frameworks.

CREATING INCLUSIVE DIABETES CARE

Healthcare providers can improve LGBTQ+ diabetes care through several practices.
Using correct names and pronouns and including gender identity in medical records is
fundamental. Training staff on LGBTQ+ health competency reduces barriers. Using
inclusive intake forms that allow for diverse gender identities and sexual orientations
signals a welcoming environment. Connecting patients with LGBTQ+ affirming support
groups and community organizations enhances psychosocial support. Avoiding assumptions
about sexual behavior, family structure, or gender identity builds trust.

PREVENTIVE CARE CONSIDERATIONS

Cancer screening guidelines should follow organ-based rather than gender-based protocols.
Transgender men who retain breast tissue should continue breast cancer screening.
Transgender women on estrogen may develop breast tissue requiring screening. Cervical
cancer screening applies to all individuals with a cervix regardless of gender identity.
Cardiovascular risk assessment should account for both natal sex and current hormonal
status.

CONCLUSION

Equitable diabetes care for LGBTQ+ individuals requires awareness of hormone therapy
interactions, proactive mental health screening, culturally competent care delivery, and
systematic efforts to reduce healthcare barriers and discrimination.
