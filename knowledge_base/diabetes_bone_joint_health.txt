Diabetes and Bone and Joint Health: Frozen Shoulder, Carpal Tunnel, Charcot Foot, Osteoporosis
Source: ADA Musculoskeletal Complications Guidelines; EULAR Recommendations; Journal of Bone and Mineral Research

OVERVIEW

Musculoskeletal complications are among the most common yet frequently overlooked
manifestations of diabetes. Approximately 30-40% of individuals with diabetes experience
some form of musculoskeletal disorder during their lifetime. These conditions cause
significant pain, functional limitation, and reduced quality of life. The underlying mechanisms
involve glycosylation of collagen and other connective tissue proteins, microvascular disease
affecting joints and bones, neuropathy, and altered biomechanics. Early recognition and
treatment can prevent permanent disability and maintain functional independence.

FROZEN SHOULDER (ADHESIVE CAPSULITIS)

Frozen shoulder affects 10-29% of diabetic individuals compared to 2-5% of the general
population. The condition involves inflammation and fibrosis of the shoulder joint capsule
causing progressive stiffness and pain. It progresses through three stages: freezing
(increasing pain, 2-9 months), frozen (stiffness with less pain, 4-12 months), and thawing
(gradual improvement, 5-24 months). Diabetes-related frozen shoulder tends to be more
severe, bilateral, and resistant to treatment than non-diabetic cases. Treatment includes
physical therapy focusing on range of motion exercises, corticosteroid injections into the
joint capsule, hydrodilatation (saline injection to stretch the capsule), and in refractory
cases, manipulation under anesthesia or arthroscopic capsular release. Prevention involves
regular shoulder stretching and maintaining good glycemic control.

LIMITED JOINT MOBILITY (DIABETIC CHEIROARTHROPATHY)

This condition involves waxy thickening of the skin and stiffness of finger joints, affecting
8-50% of long-duration diabetic patients. The "prayer sign" test (inability to fully flatten
palms together) is a simple diagnostic tool. It results from non-enzymatic glycosylation of
collagen in tendons and periarticular tissue. Management includes glycemic optimization,
physical therapy, and hand exercises to maintain flexibility.

CARPAL TUNNEL SYNDROME

Carpal tunnel syndrome occurs in 14-30% of diabetic patients, twice the rate of the general
population. Diabetic neuropathy combined with tenosynovial swelling from glycoprotein
deposition compresses the median nerve. Symptoms include numbness, tingling, and
weakness in the thumb, index, and middle fingers, typically worse at night. Differentiation
from diabetic peripheral neuropathy is important and may require nerve conduction studies.
Treatment ranges from wrist splinting and corticosteroid injection to surgical carpal tunnel
release in severe cases. Diabetic patients have slightly lower surgical success rates than
non-diabetic patients.

CHARCOT FOOT (CHARCOT NEUROARTHROPATHY)

Charcot foot is a devastating complication occurring primarily in patients with severe
peripheral neuropathy. The foot's bone and joint structure progressively collapses due to
repeated unperceived trauma, inflammation, and bone resorption. Early signs include a warm,
swollen, red foot that is typically painless due to neuropathy. This presentation is often
misdiagnosed as infection or gout, leading to dangerous treatment delays. Acute Charcot
must be treated with immediate non-weight-bearing immobilization using a total contact cast
for 3-6 months until inflammation subsides. Failure to immobilize leads to severe deformity,
the classic "rocker-bottom" foot, which creates pressure points prone to ulceration and
amputation. Prevention through daily foot inspections, proper footwear, and regular podiatric
care is essential.

DUPUYTREN'S CONTRACTURE

This condition causes thickening and contracture of the palmar fascia, leading to inability
to straighten affected fingers. It affects 16-42% of diabetic patients. Treatment options
include collagenase injection, needle aponeurotomy, or surgical fasciectomy for severe
contracture.

TRIGGER FINGER (STENOSING TENOSYNOVITIS)

Trigger finger causes painful locking or catching of fingers during flexion, affecting 10-20%
of diabetic individuals. Corticosteroid injections provide relief in 50-60% of diabetic cases
compared to 80-90% in non-diabetic cases. Surgical release is effective when injections fail.

OSTEOPOROSIS AND FRACTURE RISK

Type 1 diabetes reduces bone mineral density and increases hip fracture risk 6-7 fold.
Type 2 diabetes maintains or increases bone density but still elevates fracture risk 1.3-2
fold due to impaired bone quality. Standard DEXA scans may underestimate fracture risk
in Type 2 diabetes. Falls prevention is critical given the high fracture rate.

CONCLUSION

Musculoskeletal complications of diabetes are common and impactful. Systematic screening,
early intervention, glycemic optimization, and physical therapy form the cornerstones of
management and can significantly improve function and quality of life.
