Physical Activity Recommendations for Diabetes
Source: ADA Standards of Medical Care 2023; WHO Global Recommendations on Physical Activity

BENEFITS OF PHYSICAL ACTIVITY IN DIABETES

Regular physical activity improves blood glucose control and can prevent or delay type 2 diabetes. Benefits include:
- Improved insulin sensitivity (lasts 24-72 hours after exercise)
- Reduced HbA1c (typically 0.5-0.7% reduction)
- Weight management and fat loss
- Reduced cardiovascular risk
- Improved blood pressure and lipid profile
- Enhanced mental health and reduced depression
- Improved sleep quality
- Reduced risk of diabetes-related complications

AEROBIC EXERCISE RECOMMENDATIONS

Adults with diabetes should perform:
- At least 150 minutes/week of moderate-intensity aerobic physical activity
- OR at least 75 minutes/week of vigorous-intensity aerobic activity
- OR an equivalent combination of moderate and vigorous activity
- Activity should be spread over at least 3 days/week, with no more than 2 consecutive days without activity

Moderate-intensity activities (50-70% of max heart rate):
- Brisk walking (5-6 km/h)
- Cycling on flat terrain
- Swimming at a moderate pace
- Dancing
- Gardening/yard work
- Tennis (doubles)

Vigorous-intensity activities (>70% of max heart rate):
- Jogging or running
- Cycling at fast pace or uphill
- Swimming laps
- Aerobic dancing
- Hiking uphill
- Tennis (singles)

RESISTANCE EXERCISE RECOMMENDATIONS

- Perform resistance training at least 2-3 times per week on non-consecutive days
- Include exercises targeting all major muscle groups
- Perform 8-10 exercises per session
- Complete 1-3 sets of 10-15 repetitions for each exercise
- Progressively increase weight/resistance over time

Benefits specific to resistance training:
- Increased muscle mass improves glucose disposal
- Enhanced resting metabolic rate
- Improved body composition
- Better functional capacity and fall prevention in older adults
- Can reduce HbA1c by 0.3-0.5% independently of aerobic exercise

FLEXIBILITY AND BALANCE EXERCISES

- Stretching exercises 2-3 times per week
- Yoga and tai chi can improve flexibility, balance, and glycemic control
- Particularly important for older adults to reduce fall risk
- May also provide stress reduction benefits

EXERCISE PRECAUTIONS FOR PEOPLE WITH DIABETES

Blood glucose management during exercise:
- Check blood glucose before exercise
- If glucose < 100 mg/dL (5.6 mmol/L): consume 15-30 g carbohydrate before exercise
- If glucose > 250 mg/dL (13.9 mmol/L) with ketones: avoid vigorous activity
- Carry fast-acting glucose source during exercise
- Monitor for delayed hypoglycemia (can occur up to 24 hours post-exercise)

Special considerations:
- People on insulin or sulfonylureas: higher risk of hypoglycemia
- Peripheral neuropathy: proper footwear, avoid activities that increase risk of foot injury
- Autonomic neuropathy: monitor for signs of cardiac dysfunction
- Retinopathy (proliferative): avoid high-intensity or jarring activities
- Start slowly and progress gradually if previously sedentary

REDUCING SEDENTARY BEHAVIOR

- Break up prolonged sitting every 30 minutes with light activity (3 minutes of standing, walking, or light activity)
- Reducing sedentary time improves glycemic control even without structured exercise
- Standing desks and walking meetings are practical strategies
- Total daily movement (steps) contributes to glucose control independent of structured exercise
- Aim for ≥ 7,000-10,000 steps/day when possible

EXERCISE TIMING

- Post-meal exercise (within 1-3 hours after eating) is particularly effective for reducing postprandial glucose spikes
- Morning exercise may improve fasting glucose levels
- Consistency in timing helps with glycemic predictability
- Any time is better than no exercise
