WHO - Diabetes Overview
Source: World Health Organization (WHO), 2023

DEFINITION AND TYPES

Diabetes is a chronic, metabolic disease characterized by elevated levels of blood glucose (or blood sugar), which leads over time to serious damage to the heart, blood vessels, eyes, kidneys and nerves. The most common is type 2 diabetes, usually in adults, which occurs when the body becomes resistant to insulin or doesn't make enough insulin. In the past three decades the prevalence of type 2 diabetes has risen dramatically in countries of all income levels.

Type 1 diabetes, once known as juvenile diabetes or insulin-dependent diabetes, is a chronic condition in which the pancreas produces little or no insulin by itself. For people living with diabetes, access to affordable treatment, including insulin, is critical to their survival.

Gestational diabetes is hyperglycaemia with blood glucose values above normal but below those diagnostic of diabetes, occurring during pregnancy. Women with gestational diabetes are at an increased risk of complications during pregnancy and at delivery. These women and possibly their children are also at increased risk of type 2 diabetes in the future.

GLOBAL BURDEN

The number of people with diabetes rose from 108 million in 1980 to 422 million in 2014, and to approximately 537 million in 2021. The global prevalence of diabetes among adults over 18 years of age rose from 4.7% in 1980 to 8.5% in 2014 and continues to rise. Diabetes prevalence has been rising more rapidly in low- and middle-income countries than in high-income countries.

Diabetes is a major cause of blindness, kidney failure, heart attacks, stroke and lower limb amputation. Between 2000 and 2019, there was a 3% increase in age-standardized mortality rates from diabetes. In 2019, diabetes and kidney disease due to diabetes caused an estimated 2 million deaths.

PREVENTION

A healthy diet, regular physical activity, maintaining a normal body weight and avoiding tobacco use are ways to prevent or delay the onset of type 2 diabetes. Diabetes can be treated and its consequences avoided or delayed with diet, physical activity, medication and regular screening and treatment for complications.

DIAGNOSIS

Early diagnosis can be accomplished through relatively inexpensive testing of blood glucose. People with type 1 diabetes need insulin injections for survival. One of the most important ways to treat type 2 diabetes is to keep a healthy lifestyle.

RISK FACTORS FOR TYPE 2 DIABETES

Several risk factors have been associated with type 2 diabetes and include:
- Family history of diabetes
- Overweight and obesity
- Physical inactivity
- Age (risk increases with age, especially after 45)
- High blood pressure
- Ethnicity (higher rates in certain ethnic groups)
- History of gestational diabetes
- Polycystic ovary syndrome
- Impaired glucose tolerance (IGT)

SCREENING RECOMMENDATIONS

WHO recommends that screening for diabetes and associated risk factors be integrated into primary health care. Early detection and management of diabetes can prevent or delay the serious complications of diabetes.
