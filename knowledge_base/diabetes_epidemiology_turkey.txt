Diabetes Epidemiology in Turkey and Global Context
Sources: TURDEP-I (1998), TURDEP-II (2010), IDF Diabetes Atlas 10th Edition 2021, WHO NCD Country Profiles, Turkish Society of Endocrinology and Metabolism (TEMD), TEKHARF Study

TURKEY - NATIONAL DATA

TURDEP Studies (Turkish Diabetes Epidemiology Studies):

TURDEP-I (1997-1998):
- First nationwide diabetes prevalence study in Turkey
- 24,788 participants aged ≥20 years from 540 centers
- Results: Diabetes prevalence 7.2%, IGT prevalence 6.7%
- Total dysglycemia: 13.9%
- Marked urban-rural difference: Higher prevalence in urban areas

TURDEP-II (2010):
- Follow-up national study, 26,499 participants
- Results: Diabetes prevalence 13.7% (90% increase in 12 years)
- Prediabetes prevalence: 30.8%
- Total dysglycemia: 44.5% (nearly half of all adults)
- Previously undiagnosed diabetes: 45% (nearly half of diabetics were unaware)
- Obesity prevalence: 36% (increased from 22% in TURDEP-I)
- Hypertension: 31.7%

Key findings from TURDEP-II:
- Women had higher diabetes prevalence than men (14.6% vs 12.4%)
- Strong association with obesity (BMI >30) and central obesity
- Diabetes prevalence increased with age: <40 years (4%), 40-59 (17%), ≥60 (30%)
- Regional variation: Highest in Southeast Turkey and Mediterranean regions
- Education inversely correlated with diabetes risk
- Estimated 6.5 million adults with diabetes in Turkey (2010)

Current Estimates (2021-2025):
- IDF estimates approximately 9-10 million adults with diabetes in Turkey
- Turkey ranks among the top 10 countries in Europe for diabetes prevalence
- Estimated healthcare expenditure on diabetes: $10-12 billion annually
- Projected to exceed 12 million diabetes patients by 2045

TEKHARF Study (Turkish Adult Risk Factor Study):
- Longitudinal cohort study following Turkish adults since 1990
- Diabetes incidence: Approximately 1% per year (new cases)
- Metabolic syndrome prevalence: 40% in women, 28% in men
- Cardiovascular mortality 2-3x higher in diabetic participants

Risk Factors Specific to Turkish Population:
- High prevalence of central obesity (visceral fat distribution)
- Dietary transition: Shift from traditional Mediterranean/Anatolian diet to Western diet
- Rapid urbanization and sedentary lifestyle
- High carbohydrate diet (bread consumption among highest in the world)
- Genetic predisposition: Turkish population has intermediate risk between European and South Asian populations
- Cultural factors: Large portion sizes, sugar-rich traditional desserts, limited health literacy in rural areas
- Consanguineous marriages in Eastern Turkey may amplify genetic risk

GLOBAL EPIDEMIOLOGY

IDF Diabetes Atlas 10th Edition (2021) Key Statistics:

Current burden:
- 537 million adults (20-79 years) living with diabetes globally
- 1 in 10 adults has diabetes
- 240 million (44.7%) are undiagnosed
- 6.7 million deaths attributed to diabetes in 2021
- $966 billion in health expenditure (11% of total global health spending)

Type distribution:
- Type 2 diabetes: ~90-95% of all cases
- Type 1 diabetes: ~5-10%
- Gestational diabetes: Affects 21 million pregnancies annually

Regional prevalence (age-adjusted):
- Middle East and North Africa (MENA): 16.2% (highest)
- South-East Asia: 90 million people (largest absolute number)
- North America and Caribbean: 14.0%
- Europe: 9.2% (61 million people)
- Western Pacific: 206 million people
- Africa: 24 million people (60% undiagnosed — highest rate)

Top 10 countries by number of adults with diabetes (2021):
1. China: 141 million
2. India: 74 million
3. Pakistan: 33 million
4. USA: 32 million
5. Indonesia: 19 million
6. Brazil: 16 million
7. Mexico: 14 million
8. Germany: 12 million
9. Turkey: 10 million
10. Japan: 10 million

Projections for 2045:
- 783 million adults projected to have diabetes (46% increase)
- Greatest increase expected in Africa (134%) and South-East Asia (68%)
- Low- and middle-income countries will bear disproportionate burden

Prediabetes:
- 541 million adults with IGT globally
- By 2045, projected to reach 730 million
- Annual conversion rate to diabetes without intervention: 5-10%

SOCIOECONOMIC IMPACT

Direct costs:
- Diabetes accounts for 10-15% of national healthcare budgets in most countries
- People with diabetes have 2-3x higher healthcare expenditure than those without
- Hospitalization costs are the largest component (30-40%)
- Medication costs rising due to newer, more expensive therapies (GLP-1 RAs, SGLT2i, CGM)

Indirect costs:
- Reduced productivity (absenteeism and presenteeism)
- Early retirement and disability
- Premature mortality (working-age deaths)
- Caregiver burden
- Estimated indirect costs equal or exceed direct costs

Health system challenges:
- Shortage of endocrinologists globally (especially in low-income countries)
- Limited access to essential medicines (insulin, glucose test strips) in many settings
- Insulin affordability crisis: Despite being discovered 100+ years ago, insulin prices remain prohibitively high in many countries
- Need for integrated care models: Primary care-based diabetes management with specialist support

PREVENTION AT POPULATION LEVEL

Evidence-based strategies:
- Sugar-sweetened beverage taxes (Mexico, UK, South Africa — shown to reduce consumption by 10-20%)
- Front-of-pack nutrition labeling (Chile model: significant impact on consumer choices)
- Urban planning for physical activity (walkable cities, cycling infrastructure)
- School-based nutrition and physical activity programs
- National diabetes prevention programs (CDC model in USA, NHS DPP in UK)
- Workplace wellness programs
- Public awareness campaigns targeting modifiable risk factors
- Integration of diabetes screening into primary care routine

WHO Global Targets for NCDs:
- Halt the rise in diabetes and obesity by 2025 (not on track)
- 80% availability of essential NCD medicines including insulin by 2025
- 25% reduction in premature mortality from NCDs by 2025
