Global and Turkey Diabetes Statistics: Prevalence, Trends, and 2030 Projections
Source: IDF Diabetes Atlas 10th Edition; TEMD National Diabetes Survey; WHO Global Diabetes Report; TUIK Health Statistics

GLOBAL DIABETES PREVALENCE 2024

As of 2024, approximately 537 million adults aged 20-79 are living with diabetes worldwide,
representing 10.5% of the global adult population. This number has more than tripled since
2000, when an estimated 151 million adults had diabetes. An additional 541 million adults
have impaired glucose tolerance (prediabetes) and are at high risk of developing Type 2
diabetes. Alarmingly, approximately 44% of adults with diabetes (240 million people) remain
undiagnosed. The global diabetes-related health expenditure reached $966 billion in 2021
and continues to rise. Diabetes caused approximately 6.7 million deaths in 2021, equivalent
to one death every 5 seconds.

REGIONAL DISTRIBUTION

The highest diabetes prevalence by region is in the Middle East and North Africa (MENA)
at 16.2%, followed by Southeast Asia at 11.3%. The Western Pacific region has the largest
absolute number of diabetic individuals at 206 million. Africa has the highest proportion
of undiagnosed cases at 54%. Europe has approximately 61 million diabetic adults with a
prevalence of 9.2%. The top five countries by number of diabetic adults are China (141
million), India (74 million), Pakistan (33 million), United States (32 million), and Indonesia
(19 million). Turkey ranks among the top 10 in the European region.

TURKEY SPECIFIC STATISTICS

Turkey has one of the highest diabetes prevalence rates in Europe. According to the most
recent TEMD national survey data, diabetes prevalence in Turkey is approximately 15.7%
among adults aged 20 and over, affecting approximately 9-10 million people. Prediabetes
prevalence is estimated at 14.5%, placing an additional 7-8 million people at risk. The
prevalence varies significantly by region: southeastern Turkey has the highest rates at
approximately 18-20%, while western coastal regions show lower prevalence at 12-14%.
Urban areas have slightly higher prevalence than rural areas. Women have a higher diabetes
prevalence (17.2%) compared to men (14.1%) in Turkey. Obesity, a major diabetes risk
factor, affects approximately 32% of the Turkish population, one of the highest rates in
Europe.

TYPE 1 DIABETES TRENDS

Type 1 diabetes incidence is increasing globally by approximately 3-4% annually. An
estimated 1.2 million children and adolescents live with Type 1 diabetes worldwide. In
Turkey, Type 1 diabetes incidence in children under 15 is approximately 10-12 per 100,000
per year, showing a steady increase over the past two decades. Access to insulin remains
a critical issue: the WHO estimates that only 50% of people with Type 2 diabetes who need
insulin have access to it globally, and approximately half of all insulin is consumed by just
three countries (United States, China, India).

GESTATIONAL DIABETES TRENDS

Gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) affects approximately 14-16% of pregnancies globally.
In Turkey, GDM prevalence is estimated at 8-15% depending on diagnostic criteria and
population studied. GDM rates are rising parallel to maternal obesity trends. Women with
GDM have a 50% lifetime risk of developing Type 2 diabetes.

ECONOMIC BURDEN

The total global cost of diabetes was estimated at $966 billion in 2021 and projected to
exceed $1 trillion by 2030. In Turkey, diabetes-related healthcare expenditure represents
a significant portion of the national health budget. Direct costs include medications,
monitoring supplies, hospitalizations, and managing complications. Indirect costs include
lost productivity, disability, and premature mortality. Diabetic complications drive the
majority of costs: treating a diabetic patient with complications costs 3-5 times more
than treating one without.

PROJECTIONS FOR 2030 AND BEYOND

The IDF projects that global diabetes prevalence will reach 643 million by 2030 and 783
million by 2045. For Turkey, projections estimate diabetes prevalence could reach 17-20%
by 2030 without significant intervention, potentially affecting 12-14 million adults. Key
drivers of this increase include population aging, urbanization, increasing obesity rates,
sedentary lifestyles, and dietary changes toward processed foods. The prediabetes
population represents both the greatest risk and the greatest opportunity for intervention:
lifestyle modification programs can reduce conversion to diabetes by 58%.

MORTALITY AND COMPLICATIONS DATA

Diabetes is the 9th leading direct cause of death globally. In Turkey, cardiovascular disease
accounts for approximately 50-60% of diabetes-related deaths. End-stage renal disease from
diabetes has increased by 30% in the last decade. Diabetic retinopathy affects approximately
35% of all diabetic patients globally. Lower limb amputation rates in diabetic patients
remain 10-20 times higher than in non-diabetic populations.

CONCLUSION

The global and Turkish diabetes epidemic continues to accelerate. Without aggressive
prevention strategies targeting prediabetes, obesity, and sedentary lifestyles, the
projected numbers for 2030 will place unsustainable burden on healthcare systems.
