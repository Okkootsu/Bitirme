Diabetes and Skin Conditions
Source: American Academy of Dermatology; Journal of Clinical Endocrinology; Diabetes Care

PREVALENCE AND OVERVIEW

Skin conditions affect up to 79% of people with diabetes at some point. High blood sugar impairs circulation, damages nerves, and weakens immune function, all of which affect skin health. Some skin conditions can even be the first sign of undiagnosed diabetes. Recognizing these conditions early helps with both skin treatment and diabetes management.

DIABETES-SPECIFIC SKIN CONDITIONS

Acanthosis nigricans: Dark, velvety patches typically in skin folds (neck, armpits, groin). Strongly associated with insulin resistance. Often appears before diabetes diagnosis. Weight loss and improved insulin sensitivity can reduce it. Not harmful itself but signals metabolic problems.

Diabetic dermopathy (shin spots): Light brown, scaly patches on the shins. Most common diabetic skin condition (up to 55% of patients). Caused by changes in small blood vessels. Usually painless and harmless. No specific treatment needed.

Necrobiosis lipoidica: Rare but distinctive: shiny, reddish-brown patches, usually on shins. Can become ulcerated and painful. More common in women. Treatment includes topical steroids and wound care if ulcerated.

Diabetic blisters (bullosis diabeticorum): Painless blisters on hands, feet, legs, or forearms. Resemble burn blisters. Usually heal on their own in 2-5 weeks. Do not pop: risk of infection.

Digital sclerosis: Thick, waxy, tight skin on fingers and toes. Can limit joint mobility (prayer sign: unable to press palms flat together). Associated with long-standing, poorly controlled diabetes.

INFECTION-RELATED SKIN PROBLEMS

Bacterial infections: Staphylococcal infections (boils, styes, carbuncles) are more common and more severe. Cellulitis risk is increased. Treat promptly with antibiotics. Keep wounds clean and covered.

Fungal infections: Candida (yeast) infections: common in skin folds, under nails, and genital areas. Athlete's foot and jock itch are more persistent. Ringworm may be harder to treat. Antifungal treatments work but may take longer.

Itching (pruritus): Localized itching may indicate fungal infection or dry skin. Generalized itching can signal poor circulation or kidney complications. Moisturize regularly. Avoid very hot showers.

SKIN CARE ROUTINE FOR DIABETIC PATIENTS

Daily care:
- Keep skin clean and dry, especially in skin folds
- Moisturize daily with fragrance-free lotion (avoid between toes)
- Use mild, pH-balanced soap
- Pat skin dry, do not rub
- Check entire body daily for cuts, blisters, redness, or changes
- Treat cuts immediately: clean with soap and water, apply antibiotic ointment, cover with bandage
- Avoid scratching: can lead to open wounds and infections

Prevention:
- Maintain blood sugar control (best prevention for all skin conditions)
- Stay hydrated (minimum 8 glasses of water daily)
- Wear cotton clothing that allows skin to breathe
- Use sunscreen (diabetic skin is more susceptible to sun damage)
- Humidifier in winter to prevent dry skin
- See a dermatologist annually or immediately for non-healing wounds
