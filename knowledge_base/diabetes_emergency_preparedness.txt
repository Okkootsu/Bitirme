Diabetes Emergency Preparedness: Disasters, Medication Stockpiling, Insulin Storage
Source: ADA Disaster Preparedness Guidelines; FEMA Diabetes Emergency Planning; CDC Emergency Health Resources

OVERVIEW

Natural disasters, power outages, and emergencies pose unique threats to individuals with
diabetes. Disruption of medication access, loss of refrigeration for insulin, inability to
monitor blood glucose, irregular meals, and extreme stress can all lead to life-threatening
complications. Turkey is located in one of the most seismically active regions in the world,
with the devastating 2023 Kahramanmaras earthquakes displacing millions and disrupting
healthcare access. The 1999 Marmara earthquake similarly highlighted vulnerabilities for
chronic disease patients. Every diabetic individual should have an emergency preparedness
plan that can sustain their management for at least 7-14 days.

EMERGENCY SUPPLY KIT

A comprehensive diabetes emergency kit should contain the following items stored in a
waterproof, easily portable container. Medications: minimum 2-week supply of all diabetes
medications, extra insulin vials or pens, oral medications in original labeled containers.
Monitoring supplies: glucometer with extra batteries, test strips (at least 100), lancets,
ketone test strips for Type 1 diabetes patients. Hypoglycemia treatment: glucose tablets,
juice boxes, hard candy, glucagon emergency kit. Medical documentation: list of all
medications with doses, healthcare provider contact information, insurance cards, medical
ID bracelet or card, brief medical history. Food supplies: non-perishable diabetes-friendly
foods including nuts, peanut butter, crackers, canned beans, and water (at least 1 gallon
per person per day for 3 days).

INSULIN STORAGE DURING POWER OUTAGES

Insulin storage is one of the most critical concerns during emergencies. Unopened insulin
should be refrigerated at 36-46 degrees Fahrenheit (2-8 degrees Celsius). Once opened,
most insulin can be kept at room temperature (below 86F or 30C) for 28-42 days depending
on the type. During power outages, do not freeze insulin as frozen insulin loses effectiveness
permanently. Use insulated bags with cool packs to maintain temperature. Evaporative
cooling methods (wet cloth around insulin in a ventilated area) can help in hot environments.
Insulin that has been exposed to temperatures above 98F (37C) should be discarded. In
Turkey, summer temperatures frequently exceed 40C in southeastern regions, making
insulin protection especially critical. If unsure whether insulin has been compromised, it
may appear cloudy (if normally clear), have particles, or show discoloration.

MEDICATION STOCKPILING STRATEGIES

Maintaining an emergency medication supply requires planning. Request 90-day supplies
when possible rather than 30-day prescriptions. In Turkey, SGK typically allows 1-month
supplies but physicians can prescribe longer supplies with justification. Rotate stock by
using oldest medications first and replacing with new prescriptions. Store medications in a
cool, dry location in the emergency kit. Keep a written record of expiration dates and check
quarterly. Some pharmacies offer automatic refill programs that help maintain supply.
Never let medication supply drop below a 1-week reserve.

MANAGING DIABETES DURING NATURAL DISASTERS

During an active disaster, stress hormones raise blood glucose significantly, potentially
by 100-200 mg/dL. If monitoring is unavailable, take medications on schedule and eat
regularly if possible. Physical activity from emergency situations (walking, carrying
supplies) affects blood glucose unpredictably. If rationing insulin becomes necessary,
prioritize long-acting or basal insulin over rapid-acting to prevent DKA. Seek medical
assistance at disaster relief stations or field hospitals as soon as possible. AFAD
(Turkey's Disaster and Emergency Management Authority) coordinates medical supplies
including insulin at disaster sites.

COMMUNICATION AND EVACUATION PLANNING

Develop an evacuation plan that includes diabetes supplies. Inform family members and
neighbors about your diabetes emergency needs. Program emergency contacts into your
phone and keep a written copy. Know the locations of nearby hospitals and pharmacies
along evacuation routes. Register with local emergency services as a medically vulnerable
individual. Keep emergency supply kit near the door or in the car for quick access.

EXTREME WEATHER CONSIDERATIONS

Heat waves increase dehydration risk and insulin absorption rate. Cold weather can cause
insulin to freeze if stored improperly and affects glucometer accuracy. Flooding contaminates
water supplies and can destroy medication stores. Earthquakes may trap individuals away
from their supplies, reinforcing the need for supplies in multiple locations.

CONCLUSION

Emergency preparedness is not optional for individuals with diabetes. A well-stocked
emergency kit, insulin storage plan, and communication strategy can prevent diabetic
emergencies during disasters and potentially save lives.
