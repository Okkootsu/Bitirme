Geriatric Diabetes Management (Adults ≥65 Years)
Sources: ADA Standards of Care 2024, Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline 2019, EDWPOP Position Statement

EPIDEMIOLOGY

- More than 25% of adults ≥65 years have diabetes
- Fastest-growing demographic with diabetes worldwide
- Prevalence in nursing homes: 25-35%
- Diabetes in elderly is associated with higher rates of premature death, functional disability, and coexisting illnesses (hypertension, coronary heart disease, stroke)
- In Turkey, diabetes prevalence in adults ≥60 years is approximately 30% (TURDEP-II)

UNIQUE CHALLENGES IN OLDER ADULTS

1. Heterogeneity: Older adults with diabetes range from healthy and independent to frail with multiple comorbidities
2. Polypharmacy: Average elderly diabetic takes 7-10 medications; drug interactions and adverse effects are common
3. Cognitive decline: Diabetes increases dementia risk by 50-100%; both hyperglycemia and hypoglycemia accelerate cognitive decline
4. Fall risk: Hypoglycemia, neuropathy, polypharmacy, and orthostatic hypotension all increase fall risk
5. Sarcopenia: Loss of muscle mass worsens insulin resistance and functional capacity
6. Social isolation: May impair self-management, meal preparation, medication adherence
7. Depression: Prevalence 2-3x higher than in non-diabetic elderly

ADA FRAMEWORK FOR GLYCEMIC TARGETS

1. Healthy (few coexisting chronic illnesses, intact cognitive and functional status):
   - HbA1c target: <7.0-7.5%
   - Fasting glucose: 80-130 mg/dL
   - Bedtime glucose: 80-180 mg/dL
   - Rationale: Longer life expectancy, can benefit from tighter control

2. Complex/Intermediate (multiple coexisting chronic illnesses, 2+ ADL impairments, mild-moderate cognitive impairment):
   - HbA1c target: <8.0%
   - Fasting glucose: 90-150 mg/dL
   - Bedtime glucose: 100-180 mg/dL
   - Rationale: Hypoglycemia risk outweighs benefits of tight control

3. Very Complex/Poor Health (end-stage chronic illness, moderate-severe cognitive impairment, 2+ ADL dependencies, long-term care facility):
   - HbA1c target: <8.5% (avoid HbA1c >9%)
   - Fasting glucose: 100-180 mg/dL
   - Bedtime glucose: 110-200 mg/dL
   - Rationale: Avoid symptomatic hyperglycemia and any hypoglycemia; comfort is priority

MEDICATION CONSIDERATIONS

Preferred agents in elderly:
- Metformin: Generally safe; avoid if eGFR <30; watch for B12 deficiency
- DPP-4 inhibitors: Low hypoglycemia risk, weight neutral, well tolerated
- GLP-1 receptor agonists: CV and renal benefits; may cause nausea, weight loss (caution in frail elderly)
- SGLT2 inhibitors: CV and renal benefits; risk of dehydration, UTI, euglycemic DKA; caution in very elderly

Agents to use with caution:
- Sulfonylureas: HIGH hypoglycemia risk; avoid glyburide entirely; if needed, use short-acting (glipizide)
- Insulin: Simplify regimens; consider once-daily basal insulin (glargine, degludec); avoid complex basal-bolus in cognitively impaired patients
- Thiazolidinediones: Fluid retention, heart failure exacerbation, fracture risk

DEINTENSIFICATION OF THERAPY

- Actively consider deintensification (reducing medications) when:
  * HbA1c is well below individualized target
  * Patient has experienced hypoglycemia
  * Health status has declined
  * Treatment burden is excessive
  * Life expectancy is limited
- Deintensification is an active clinical decision, not neglect
- Stepwise approach: Remove sulfonylureas first, reduce insulin doses, simplify regimen

HYPOGLYCEMIA IN ELDERLY

- Hypoglycemia is the most dangerous acute complication in elderly diabetics
- Consequences: Falls, fractures (hip fracture risk 70% higher), cardiac arrhythmias, seizures, cognitive decline, death
- Hypoglycemia unawareness more common in elderly (impaired counterregulatory response)
- All elderly patients on insulin or sulfonylureas should have glucose tablets and glucagon available
- CGM (continuous glucose monitoring) increasingly used in elderly for hypoglycemia detection

COGNITIVE SCREENING

- Screen for cognitive impairment at diagnosis and periodically
- Tools: Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE), Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA), Mini-Cog
- Cognitive impairment affects ability to: manage medications, recognize hypoglycemia symptoms, prepare meals, perform glucose monitoring
- Simplify self-management tasks; involve caregivers in diabetes education

NURSING HOME AND LONG-TERM CARE

- Sliding-scale insulin alone is NOT recommended (reactive, not proactive)
- Basal insulin with correction doses is preferred
- Consistent carbohydrate meal plans rather than restrictive diabetic diets
- Avoid overly restrictive diets that reduce quality of life and caloric intake
- Regular glucose monitoring schedule
- Staff education on hypoglycemia recognition and treatment

SCREENING FOR COMPLICATIONS

- Continue screening for complications that would benefit from intervention
- Consider discontinuing screening when treatment would not change (e.g., retinal screening in patient with limited life expectancy and no symptoms)
- Annual foot exam remains important (neuropathy, ulcers, fall prevention)
- Blood pressure target: <130/80 if tolerated; avoid aggressive lowering (orthostatic hypotension)
- Statin therapy: Continue in established ASCVD; initiation in primary prevention is less clear after age 75

EXERCISE IN ELDERLY WITH DIABETES

- Balance and flexibility exercises to prevent falls (tai chi, yoga)
- Resistance training to combat sarcopenia (2-3 sessions/week)
- Aerobic activity as tolerated (walking, swimming, cycling)
- Supervised exercise programs preferred
- Check feet before and after exercise
- Adjust medications to prevent exercise-induced hypoglycemia
