Resistance Training and Strength Exercise for Diabetes
Source: ADA/ACSM Joint Position Statement on Exercise; Diabetes Care Journal 2023; Colberg et al. Systematic Review

BENEFITS OF RESISTANCE TRAINING IN DIABETES

Resistance training, also known as strength training or weight training, has emerged as a critical component of diabetes management alongside aerobic exercise. A 2023 meta-analysis of 45 trials demonstrated that resistance training alone reduces HbA1c by 0.34 percentage points, while combined resistance and aerobic training reduces HbA1c by 0.74 percentage points. Resistance exercise increases skeletal muscle mass, which is the primary site of insulin-mediated glucose uptake, accounting for approximately 80 percent of postprandial glucose disposal. A single session of resistance training can improve insulin sensitivity for 24 to 72 hours post-exercise.

MECHANISMS OF BLOOD SUGAR IMPROVEMENT

During resistance exercise, muscle contractions activate GLUT4 glucose transporters independent of insulin, allowing glucose to enter muscle cells through a non-insulin-dependent pathway. This is particularly valuable in type 2 diabetes where insulin resistance impairs the normal GLUT4 translocation. Increased muscle mass from chronic resistance training elevates basal metabolic rate by approximately 7 percent per kilogram of added muscle, creating a larger glucose disposal reservoir. Resistance training also reduces intramuscular lipid accumulation, which is a known contributor to insulin resistance.

RECOMMENDED TRAINING PROTOCOLS

The ADA recommends resistance training at least 2 to 3 non-consecutive days per week, targeting all major muscle groups: chest, back, shoulders, arms, core, and legs. Each session should include 8 to 10 exercises performed for 1 to 3 sets of 10 to 15 repetitions at moderate intensity (50 to 75 percent of one-repetition maximum). Beginners should start with one set of 15 repetitions using light resistance and progress gradually over 4 to 8 weeks. Rest periods of 60 to 90 seconds between sets allow adequate recovery without excessive blood pressure elevation.

SAFE ROUTINES FOR BEGINNERS

A recommended beginner routine includes: seated chest press, lat pulldown, shoulder press, bicep curls, tricep extensions, leg press, leg curls, calf raises, and abdominal crunches using resistance machines for safety. Resistance bands are an excellent alternative for home-based programs, offering variable resistance without requiring heavy equipment. Bodyweight exercises such as wall push-ups, chair squats, and standing calf raises are appropriate starting points for deconditioned individuals. Proper form must be prioritized over load to prevent musculoskeletal injuries.

BLOOD SUGAR RESPONSE DURING AND AFTER TRAINING

Blood glucose may initially rise during high-intensity resistance exercise due to hepatic glucose output stimulated by catecholamines and cortisol, particularly in sets performed to muscular failure. This transient hyperglycemia typically resolves within 1 to 2 hours as muscles replenish glycogen stores. Individuals on insulin should be aware that resistance training can cause delayed hypoglycemia 6 to 12 hours after exercise. Blood glucose monitoring before, immediately after, and several hours post-exercise is advised during the first weeks of a new program.

SPECIAL POPULATIONS AND PRECAUTIONS

Individuals with proliferative retinopathy should avoid heavy lifting, Valsalva maneuvers, and positions with the head below the waist due to risk of vitreous hemorrhage. Those with peripheral neuropathy should avoid exercises that place excessive stress on insensate feet. Patients with autonomic neuropathy may have impaired heart rate and blood pressure responses, requiring modified intensity monitoring. Individuals with uncontrolled hypertension (systolic above 160 mmHg) should delay resistance training until blood pressure is controlled.

PROGRESSIVE OVERLOAD AND LONG-TERM PLANNING

Training adaptations require progressive increases in resistance, volume, or exercise complexity every 2 to 4 weeks. A periodized program alternating between hypertrophy phases (3 sets of 10 to 12 reps), strength phases (3 to 4 sets of 6 to 8 reps), and endurance phases (2 sets of 15 to 20 reps) prevents plateaus and sustains metabolic benefits. Long-term resistance training over 12 months has been shown to reduce visceral fat by 10 to 15 percent, independent of changes in body weight, with significant improvements in metabolic syndrome markers.

FREQUENCY AND COMBINATION WITH AEROBIC EXERCISE

Optimal outcomes are achieved when resistance training is combined with aerobic exercise, totaling at least 150 minutes of moderate activity per week. Performing aerobic exercise before resistance training may slightly improve fat oxidation, while resistance before aerobic exercise may better preserve muscle glycogen. No more than two consecutive days should pass without some form of physical activity. Supervision by a certified exercise physiologist or diabetes educator during the initial 8 to 12 weeks significantly improves technique, adherence, and outcomes.
